## What this skill is

`derive-paper-structure` is the sixth Cycle-1 stage-skill, owning cycle-template stage 11 (architecture spec §4.2). It receives a student whose Paper Structure Reference Articles are selected and in hand (stage 10), and walks them through a **joint comparative read** of those models' skeletons to produce `paper_structure.md` — created in Cycle 1, extended in later cycles. By completion the student has:

- **Practiced skeleton-reading on real papers** — the comparison stage 10 promised ("our reading of them will focus on: what sections they have, what goes in their methods section, how they lay out results") actually performed, jointly.
- **`paper_structure.md` written**: one row per paper section, carrying the section's purpose, which structure model informs it, its internal organization (for the structured sections), its page budget, and its agent permissions.

**What is derived and what is already fixed.** The paper's macro-structure is **not** this skill's to invent — spec §5.2 fixes it: Abstract → Research Problem → per-cycle blocks (Research Question paragraphs → Literature Review and Synthesis → Gap → Hypotheses/Expected Findings → Methods → Results → Discussion) → Conclusion. What the structure models inform is one level down: the **internal organization of the structured sections** (spec §5.1) — what a methods section for *this kind of study* contains and in what order (data, setting, empirical specification…), how results are organized and presented (lead table, estimate narrative, figures), what the discussion covers and in what sequence — plus the **in-paper titles** for the foundational sections, which the upstream skills deliberately deferred to this stage ("the final in-paper section title defers to stage 11" — that promise, and only that, is discharged here: the title is recorded as row metadata; the sections' prose and order are untouched). A student's derived structure is the architecture's skeleton fleshed out in their models' conventions.

**The register is a joint read** (educator ruling 2026-06-07) — deliberately unlike stage 10's mentor-selects logistics, because here the comparison *is* the learning. The mentor structures the walk and the student does the noticing: the mentor opens the models' text copies side by side and asks the comparative questions ("What does each methods section do first? What do both papers put in a table versus prose?"); the student names what converges and what differs; the mentor records the derived rows. Where the models converge, that's the convention and the template takes it; where they diverge, the student picks by fit to their own study, with the mentor's one-line view. Socratic-didactic blend, with the mode set by Moment 1.

**The structure-buys-no-edge register** (decision-log 2026-06-07): the mentor presents structure as the craft floor — it removes the looking-amateur negative and never adds competitive distinction. The skill never promises or implies advantage from structure; the edge lives in the gap (stage 8) and the push (stage 12).

## The permission vocabulary (educator-ruled 2026-06-07)

`paper_structure.md`'s permissions column uses a **fixed vocabulary, uniform defaults across all sections** — the column exists so the integrity rule is explicit, machine-readable (the `paper-walkthrough` hands each row to `scaffold-section` as `paper_structure_entry`, contract §4.3), teachable (Lesson 13 walks students through reading it), and extensible (per-section exceptions are possible later without redesign). Four verbs, granted per agent:

- **scaffold** — may produce a section skeleton/prompt structure in place for the student to fill (the paper skills' lane)
- **demonstrate** — may show how published authors handle a given kind of passage, via direct, attributed quotes from the structure models' own sections (decision-history §8.1 discipline; AI-composed illustrations generic and fallback-only, never entering the paper)
- **verify** — may check facts, numbers, citations, and consistency against sources and the workspace
- **comment** — may critique and discuss in conversation

**Uniform defaults, every section:** project-mentor: scaffold, demonstrate, comment. Research agent: verify, comment. Review agent: comment. **And written into every row, the universal rule: prose is student-only** — the sentences that enter the paper are the student's own; quotes from models appear only as properly cited quotations. The defaults are identical across sections by design; the vocabulary is the contract the paper skills and the other agents' Tier 2/3 authoring are bound to (obligations register **X2** — the verb binding, recorded with this draft per the register's same-session rule; nomenclature parallels in R9/V4).

**The column's true scope, stated honestly.** The column is *binding* on the **paper skill** — `scaffold-section` receives its row as `paper_structure_entry` (contract §4.3) and obeys it — and on the Tier 2/3 agents at their paper-adjacent work (the verify/comment lanes). For the **foundational sections**, the column is a *record*, not a control surface: those sections were written by stage-skills (`design-project` through `identify-gap`) that never read `paper_structure.md` and could not — the file doesn't exist until stage 11. Their rows document the integrity posture those skills already enforce by their own SOUL and contract grounding (the same posture, which is why the uniform defaults are honest). And `compile-working-paper` is **outside the per-row column entirely** — it is a whole-file reader that assembles and comments across all rows, operating above the row level; assembly is not a per-section permission and needs no fifth verb (the fixed vocabulary stands).

## The skill's internal phases

**Phase 0 — Full-text precondition (mechanical, silent when clean).** The joint read is full-text work, so the skill verifies before opening: every selected structure model has its PDF in `articles/` and its extraction in `articles/text/`. A missing `.md` with the PDF present is regenerated on the spot (`fetch-articles --convert-only`); a missing PDF is a real gap — surfaced plainly, repaired via the stage-10 capture path (fetch, or the student-obtains route) before the read proceeds, with one extra line if it arrives *unflagged*: stage 10's capture should have fetched it and the Friday audit should have caught its absence, so an unflagged missing PDF here means the corpus audit missed it — worth noting in the return payload's `advisory_notes`, not just repairing silently. The standing guarantee that makes this check normally a no-op is the **Friday corpus-integrity audit** (obligations register V1c, decision-log 2026-06-07): the review agent's weekly sweep verifies the whole claimed corpus and flags missing PDFs to the mentor's next session, so stage 11 usually arrives with the corpus already verified days earlier.

**Phase 1 — The joint skeleton read (Moment 1; the teaching conversation).** The mentor opens the structure models' text copies (`articles/text/`) and walks them side by side, one structured section at a time: methods first ("Walk through each paper's methods section with me — what does each one do first? What comes next?"), then results ("What does each paper put in its first table? What gets prose and what gets a figure?"), then discussion. The student names what the models share and where they differ; the mentor supplies the craft vocabulary as things are noticed (a *specification* subsection, a *robustness* table) — taught at the moment of noticing, not as a lecture. The foundational sections get a lighter pass: how the models title and arrange their fronts informs the in-paper titles for the already-written sections.

**Phase 2 — Derivation.** Convergences become the template; divergences are resolved by fit to the student's own study, student's pick with the mentor's one-line view. Each section gets its row: **purpose** (one line — what job this section does for the reader), **informed by** (which model, by slug), **internal organization** (the subsection/content sequence, for structured sections), and **page budget**. The budget column (educator rulings 2026-06-07, twice that date — the second supersedes the ceiling framing) works against the **program's working budget: up to 25 pages at 1.5 line spacing — generous by design, never limiting for a student project**. It still handles the file's **two kinds of rows differently**: the **foundational sections already exist as prose**, so their budget entries are *measured* — the mentor counts what the research problem, question paragraphs, Literature Review and Synthesis, gap, and (later) expected-findings sections actually run — while the **structured sections are not yet written**, so their entries are *targets*, seeded from the models' own proportions and adjusted to the student's study. The sum-under-25 check **nets out what is already spent**: if the written sections measure 9 pages, the methods/results/discussion targets share the remaining 16 — useful information for stage 12, never a constraint on it. Targets are planning aids revisited at writing time, not gates — **the competition's 20-page hard cut is an end-stage editing task** (the compile/competition-framing skills' territory): students drafting to the working format will always find ways to edit down to size at the very end, and no agent length-polices a draft under the working budget.

**Two structural conventions ride the file** (the competition reference's bindings, wired here rather than left as grounding): a **figures-and-tables convention** — every figure and table carries its citation, including ones the student made (the STS rules treat a missing figure citation as disqualification-grade) — enforced downstream through the research agent's `verify` grant, which explicitly includes citations; and the **no-external-links rule** — evaluators cannot click links, so nothing in the paper's structure depends on one. Both are written into the file's header conventions block, which the paper skills inherit with their rows.

**Phase 3 — Permissions, presented (brief, didactic).** The mentor presents the permission vocabulary and the uniform defaults (Moment 2) — one pass, plain: who may scaffold, demonstrate, verify, comment, and the universal rule that the sentences are the student's. This is presentation, not negotiation; the defaults are program policy. The student's takeaway is knowing how to *read* the column, because the other agents will cite it.

**Phase 4 — Write and return.** `paper_structure.md` is created (Cycle 1) or extended (later cycles: the new cycle's structured-section rows added from the new cycle's models, prior cycles' rows standing — sealed cycles' rows are never edited). Return to the walkthrough.

## The scripted moments

Per the established convention: adapt wording and re-point examples to the student's project; preserve the core steps and their order; concise, example-driven, no rationale lectures.

### Moment 1 — Opening the joint read (Phase 1)

> *"Now we use the scaffold articles. Your paper's big shape is already set — problem, questions, literature review, gap, expected findings, methods, results, discussion. What we're deciding today is what goes inside the last three for your kind of study, and we get that by reading your structural models side by side. Let's start with the methods sections: open both, and tell me what each one does first."*

### Moment 2 — The permissions column (Phase 3)

> *"One more thing goes in this file: a column that says what each agent is allowed to do in each section — I can scaffold and show you how published papers do it, the research agent can check facts and numbers, the review agent can critique. And in every row, the same rule: the sentences are yours. The other agents read this file, so it's worth knowing what it says."*

## What this skill writes to the workspace

`paper_structure.md`: the whole file (Cycle 1) or the new cycle's rows (later cycles) — this skill is the file's **sole writer** (contract §4.6: "written by `derive-paper-structure`; read by all paper skills"). The file's format: a header stating the macro-order's source (spec §5.2), the permission vocabulary's definitions and scope statement, the **conventions block** (figures/tables carry citations — student-made ones included; no external links; the 25-page working budget — 1.5 line spacing; the competition's 20-page compression is an end-stage editing task — with the measured-vs-target budget rule) — then one row per section: *section | cycle | in-paper title | purpose | informed by (slug) | internal organization | page budget (measured or target) | permissions*. Nothing to `decisions.md` (not a rationale trigger), nothing to `working_paper.md` (stage 11 writes the plan for sections, not sections — though the foundational sections' in-paper titles recorded here discharge the upstream "defers to stage 11" promises). Return payload per contract §5.1: `completion_status`, section-granular `workspace_writes`; `newly_stale_sections` is empty in the normal case — the §7 map has no stage-11 row (a structure revision during planning stales nothing already written; the structured sections it governs are written later, in execution).

**Partial returns.** Natural pauses are the phase boundaries; the file anchor is `paper_structure.md`'s row coverage — a return with methods derived but results/discussion rows absent resumes at Phase 2 with the remaining sections. The joint read's observations live in conversation, so a resumption re-opens the models at the un-derived section rather than reconstructing lost notes; the dispatch's `prior_dispatch_return` is a hint, never the source of truth (contract §11 item 9 caveat, per the siblings).

## Edge cases

**The models disagree wholesale.** When the 2–3 skeletons share little (a clean econ-empirical next to a CS-proceedings structure), the mentor is honest about what that means: the convention signal is weak, so fit to the student's study decides more of the rows, and the mentor says which model is carrying which section and why. Divergence is information, not a failure.

**The models were imperfect to begin with.** Stage 10's fewer-than-two edge case can hand this skill nearest-genre matches flagged as imperfect — the flag travels in the stage-10 annotations, and any teacher-consult outcome from that flag travels with it. The derivation proceeds cautiously and says so: convention claims are made only where the imperfect models genuinely support them; the mentor leans more on the architecture's fixed skeleton and the discipline's defaults, and the file's rows note thinner grounding where it exists.

**The student wants to reorder the paper's fixed sections.** "Results before methods" and its cousins are **not** stage 11's to grant — the macro-order is the architecture's (spec §5.2), not a convention the models are evidence about, and no amount of persistence reroutes it here. The mentor says so plainly: the paper's big shape is fixed by the program; a student who wants to argue the shape itself takes that to the teacher as a question about the program, not to this conversation. Not advisory — this is the one hard line in the skill.

**The student wants a within-section organization the models don't support.** An unusual *internal* flow (an essay-style discussion, a methods section organized by question rather than by data-then-design) gets the honest conversation: the models are the evidence for what readers of this genre expect, and departing from convention costs reader trust that a first paper can't spare. Not a hard gate — a student who persists after the conversation gets their internal organization with the divergence noted; the teacher consult is suggested for a wholesale departure. Advisory, never a veto — the contrast with the previous case is the point: section *order* is the architecture's, section *insides* are a conversation.

**The estimates run long.** If honest per-section estimates sum well over even the 25-page working format, the mentor notes it once, as information — not a gate, and nothing is trimmed for budget at design time (educator ruling: the budget is never limiting for a student project; editing down to size is an end-of-project task students reliably manage). The note rides `advisory_notes` so the record exists when writing time comes.

**A later cycle's models imply a different internal organization.** Normal — a new method-approach often means a differently-shaped methods section. The new cycle's rows carry the new organization; prior cycles' rows are untouched (their sections are sealed with their cycles). One firewall, stated the way the sibling skills state theirs: differently-shaped cycles reflect **method change across cycles, never a core-then-push progression** — the core/push pair is the within-cycle stage-12 design, and cycles do not escalate.

**The student asks the mentor to just write the structure.** The joint read is the point — the mentor declines the shortcut in one plain line ("then you'd have the file without knowing how to read papers this way — and that's the part you need"), structures the walk tighter, and keeps the student doing the noticing. If the resistance signals genuine overload, the session splits at a phase boundary rather than converting to mentor-derives.
