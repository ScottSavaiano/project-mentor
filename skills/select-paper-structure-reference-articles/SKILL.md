## What this skill is

`select-paper-structure-reference-articles` is the fifth Cycle-1 stage-skill, owning cycle-template stage 10 (architecture spec §4.2). It receives a student whose gap section is written, and the **mentor selects** the cycle's **Paper Structure Reference Articles** — the 2–3 published papers whose structure the student's own paper will emulate — and presents them. By completion the student has:

- **The purpose, stated plainly** (Moment 1): *we need a couple of articles that can serve as a scaffold — a writing blueprint for your project and paper.* And the one new reading idea that comes with it: these papers are read for their *skeleton* (what sections they have, what each contains, how results are laid out), not their findings.
- **2–3 articles selected by the mentor** against the four criteria (below), each presented with a one-line rationale, each with full text in `articles/`, recorded in `reference_articles.md` under `## Paper Structure Reference Articles (Cycle N)` with the rationale as its annotation.

**The selection is the mentor's** (educator ruling 2026-06-06). This stage is deliberately *not* an interactive screening exercise: the student has just finished the gap conversation — the heavy designation work of the cycle — and stage 10 is logistics in service of stage 11. The mentor does the screening work itself and surfaces the result with reasons; the student's part is to hear why these papers and, if they want, name papers from their own reading to add. **Student additions are soft-gated** (educator ruling, same date): a paper the student wants on the list is *added* — no extensive back-and-forth, no swap framing, no re-screening conversation. The mentor may note in one line what the addition models well (or less well), fetches its full text, and moves on; the only mechanical constraint is full text in hand, because stage 11 cannot read a skeleton it doesn't have.

The Paper Structure Reference Articles' job is unlike the other kinds' (decision-history §7.1): the other three are mostly *compass* — they lead into the problem and the literature. This set is purely *writing model*, and specifically *structure* model: stage 11 (`derive-paper-structure`) reads these papers' skeletons to produce `paper_structure.md`, which then governs how the structured sections (methods, results, discussion) are organized. **This skill selects; it never derives.** What the student's paper structure actually is — section names, internal organization, the genre's conventions — is stage 11's conversation, not this one. The boundary keeps stage 10 light: pick the models, say why, get their full texts in hand, stop.

**The two sources, in screening order:**

1. **The student's own corpus** — the Project Reference Articles and the reading-list articles, screened via the methods inventory (which articles used the student's method on what data) and each summary's method-and-data element (checklist element 4). A paper the student has already read is the best structural model available, all else equal — familiarity makes skeleton-reading fast.
2. **The structure-models trove** — `structure-models.yaml` in the private `curriculum-articles` repository (shipped with the article library; PDFs + extracted `.md` for grepping): an **educator-maintained** manifest of blueprint-suitable articles tagged by method family, genre, venue/year, and what each skeleton models well. The manifest's `wanted:` block names known coverage gaps — the mentor reads those as unavailable, not as errors. **The mentor never edits the trove**; additions and removals are the educator's.

**The four selection criteria** (educator-ruled 2026-06-06):

1. **Method-match.** The paper reports the same kind of study the student is about to run — a difference-in-differences evaluation, a scraped-corpus analysis, an agent-based simulation. A method-matched paper's methods and results organization transfers; a method-mismatched one misleads.
2. **Genre-match.** A full-length empirical social science research paper of the shape the student is producing — not a review article, a commentary, a methods note, or a compressed brief-format piece. The skeleton has to be the skeleton of the thing being built. (This is why several strong method exemplars are deliberately absent from the trove: PNAS-brief format is a poor full-paper blueprint.)
3. **Full text in hand.** Stage 11 reads structure, and structure cannot be read from an abstract. Every selected article's full text must be present in `articles/` — fetched via `fetch-articles` for any candidate not already there. An abstract-only candidate is swapped, not tolerated.
4. **Recency.** Prefer recent papers, so the structural conventions the student inherits are current. (Canonical older papers — Card & Krueger — are usable, paired with a recent model.)

In **Cycles 2 and 3**, the skill *extends*: the prior cycle's structure models stand for their cycle's sections, and the new cycle — typically arriving with a new method-approach — gets newly selected method-matched models in its own block. One firewall, stated the way the sibling skills state theirs: new models in a new cycle reflect **method change across cycles, never a core-then-push progression** — the doable-core/push pair is the within-cycle stage-12 methodology design, and cycles do not escalate. The accumulating-sections pattern is `reference_articles.md`'s throughout (decision-history §7.1). Sealed cycles are never touched; the division of responsibility is the contract's (§6.3 and §4.6's write handles), and this skill's obligation is to confine its writes to the current cycle's section.

A future revision that wants either must edit the contract table or the meta-skill's list, not this skill.

## The skill's internal phases

**Phase 1 — The mentor screens and selects (silent work, before the moment).** Before saying anything stage-10-shaped to the student, the mentor does the screening itself: the gap section and method-approach establish what kind of study the paper will report; the methods inventory and the summaries' method-and-data elements screen the student's corpus; the trove manifest is read by its `slug`, `citation`, `method_family`, `genre`, `venue_year`, and `models_well` fields (the slug ties an entry to `articles/<slug>.pdf` for the full-text check; the citation is how a pick is presented). **A field-semantics note that matters:** every trove entry is *already* genre-screened — the educator curates only full-length empirical papers in, and the brief-format exemplars are excluded by omission. The trove's `genre` values (`full-empirical`/`epi-empirical`/`econ-empirical`/`cs-empirical`) are **disciplinary tags that inform the pick but never gate it** — an `epi-empirical` skeleton is not a mismatch for a non-epi project. Criterion 2's full-paper-vs-not screen therefore applies only to the *student-corpus* candidates (where brief-format papers genuinely appear) and to Phase 3's search finds — never as a filter on trove `genre` values. The mentor picks **2–3** against the four criteria, preferring the student's own already-read articles where they qualify, drawing from the trove where they don't, and composes a one-line rationale per pick — concrete, not generic: *"a published difference-in-differences policy evaluation whose methods and results sections are organized the way yours will need to be."*

**Phase 2 — The presentation (Moment 1).** The mentor states the purpose plainly, names the picks, and gives each its rationale — then invites additions. A student-named paper from their own reading is **added to the list** (soft gate, per the ruling above): one line on what it brings, full text fetched, done. The conversation is short by design; its teaching content is the skeleton-reading idea and the *why* of each pick, not a screening walkthrough. **The register grounds itself in the moment**: Moment 1's "our reading of them will focus on…" announces the didactic turn on its own authority (the skill does not lean on `design-project` Phase 3c's mode-shift announcement, which was scoped to the methodology stage's reference-article work, weeks upstream), and its "Because these articles are just for format and not content…" sentence reconciles mentor-selection with the SOUL's collaborative-selection promise — a promise made about the Project Reference Articles, a distinction the student doesn't carry, so the moment draws it for them. The closing's "Otherwise, let's move on" gives the moment an explicit completion beat: the student either names an addition (the mentor does the adding) or the stage moves.

**Phase 3 — The extreme case: the mentor searches (only when fewer than two qualify).** The trigger is a **genuine absence**: fewer than **2** method-adjacent, full-length empirical models across both sources — possible when the method is new to the literature and the trove's `wanted:` gaps cover that family. The trigger does *not* fire on the absence of a perfect match; a method-adjacent trove entry counts as suitable (the imperfect-models edge case below already treats adjacency as workable, and Phase 3 must not contradict it). When it fires, the **mentor itself** runs the search with its own web tools — preferring open-access venues (arXiv, NBER, ACL Anthology, university repositories — the hosts `fetch-articles` confirms serve scripted clients), since a paywalled landing page cannot become a fetched full text. Found candidates are fetched (`fetch-articles`, `--url` on-request mode; its rules travel — 403-is-final, no paywall circumvention); a promising-but-paywalled find is surfaced honestly — the mentor names the article and the legitimate route (the student or teacher obtains it; `--convert-only` ingests it) rather than failing silently. Candidates are presented in the Phase 2 register, flagged as coming from outside the curated sources. This is a fallback, not a habit. When it fires, the mentor records the trove gap in the return payload's `advisory_notes` field (contract §5.1) so the walkthrough's session record surfaces it — the educator maintains the trove, and a fired fallback is the signal an addition is wanted — **and the mentor never edits `structure-models.yaml` itself**: the manifest's add-an-entry mechanism is the educator's alone (register T3). There is no research-agent handoff at this stage (the prior draft's discovery errand is retired; register R5a struck 2026-06-06). *Platform note: this phase assumes the mentor profile carries a web-discovery tool, not merely URL fetching; asserted from Hermes's native toolset, to be pinned against the platform primer at its next verification pass (see open item 5).*

**Phase 4 — Capture.** For any selection not already in `articles/`, the full text is fetched. The selections are written to `reference_articles.md` under `## Paper Structure Reference Articles (Cycle N)`, each entry carrying its rationale as the annotation. Return to the walkthrough.

## The scripted moments

Per the established convention: adapt wording and re-point examples to the student's project; preserve the core moves and order; concise, example-driven, no rationale lectures.

### Moment 1 — The purpose and the picks (Phase 2)

> *"Your paper is about to get its structure, and the best way to get one is to borrow it from published papers that did your kind of study. That means we need two or three articles that can serve as a scaffold — a writing blueprint for your project and paper. So our reading of them will focus on: what sections they have, what goes in their methods section, how they lay out results. Because these articles are just for format and not content, they are different from the Reference Articles we chose before. For this reason I have done the picking: [the selections, each with its one-line why — e.g., 'Card & Krueger — the classic study built exactly like yours: two groups, before and after, and you can see how they walk a reader through it']. Let me know if there's a paper you want to add to this list and I will do it. Otherwise, let's move on."*

**Partial returns.** The stage normally completes in one sitting (the mentor's screening is its own work, not the student's). A return interrupted mid-capture is `partial`, and resumption is file-authoritative — if `reference_articles.md` has no Paper Structure Reference Articles section for the current cycle, the skill re-runs Phase 1's screen and resumes. The idempotency claim is grounded in **determinism**: the screen's inputs (the trove manifest, the methods inventory, the summaries, the gap section) are all stable between sessions, so a re-run reaches the same picks — a mid-fetch interruption re-decides identically and re-attempts only the missing fetches (already-fetched PDFs in `articles/` are skipped, `fetch-articles`' standing behavior). Student additions from the interrupted session are the one non-derivable input; if the conversation log carries one that the file doesn't, the mentor re-confirms it in a sentence rather than silently dropping it. The dispatch's `prior_dispatch_return` is a hint, never the source of truth (contract §11 item 9 caveat, per the siblings).

## Edge cases

**The student names a paper to add.** Added — soft gate, no extended screen. The mentor offers one line on what the paper's skeleton brings (including, honestly, if its format makes it a weaker blueprint — a brief-format paper, say), fetches the full text, and records it with the rest. Only an unobtainable full text blocks an addition, and then only because stage 11 literally cannot read it — and the block is delivered gently, in the soft gate's spirit: the mentor names the legitimate route (the student obtains the PDF themselves — library, teacher, author's page — and drops it into `articles/` for `--convert-only` ingestion) and holds the addition open rather than closing it.

**The Phase 3 search also comes up short — fewer than two suitable models exist.** Honest surface: the mentor names the situation, takes the nearest-genre match (an empirical paper of the right shape whose method is closest in kind) plus the method exemplar as imperfect models, says *why* they're imperfect, and suggests the teacher consult — advisory, never a gate (stage 10 has no consultation gate to invoke). The trove-gap note still rides the return payload. Stage 11 derives more cautiously from imperfect models; the mentor never pretends a mismatched skeleton is a matched one.

**A candidate's full text cannot be obtained.** 403-is-final (`fetch-articles`' standing rule); the candidate is swapped. No abstract-only structure models — criterion 3 is the hard screen.

**The student asks why a famous paper from their reading isn't a pick.** The genre line, taught in one breath: a review's skeleton is the skeleton of a review; a brief-format paper's skeleton is compressed past use. Strong articles can be poor blueprints — that's a fact about formats, not about the articles.

**The student wants to start imitating the models' prose.** The integrity boundary (decision-history §8.1, the SOUL): *structure* imitation is exactly what the structure models are for — section organization is convention, not authorship. *Prose* belongs to the demonstration-versus-production rule: sentences from the models may be studied, and may enter the paper only as properly cited quotations; the student's sentences are their own. The mentor draws this line the moment the question arises, in one breath, without a lecture.

**`paper_structure_articles_present` is true on dispatch.** Cycles 2–3, or a revision pass. The skill confirms what stands, screens for what the new cycle's method needs that the existing models don't cover, and extends the current cycle's section — it does not re-litigate prior cycles' selections (their sections are sealed with their cycles).

**The gap is revised after the selection.** Not this skill's gate — the §7 map (stage-8 row) stales the Paper Structure Reference Articles and the walkthrough routes the rewrite; on re-dispatch this skill re-screens the existing selections against the revised gap before extending or swapping.
