## What this skill is

`develop-research-question` is the second Cycle-1 stage-skill, owning cycle-template stages 4–6 (architecture spec §4.2). It receives a student who has a fixed research problem, a chosen method-approach, and a consolidated Type 3 reference-article set — and walks them from that grounding to the cycle's two research questions, each specific enough to study successfully and rigorously, with variables specified precisely enough to measure (the SOUL's bar, "What the work looks like"). By the time this skill completes a full run, the student has:

- An exploration of **theories, variables, datasets, and methods** mined from the Type 3 articles, recorded in `project_design.md` (stage 4).
- **Two research questions**, developed together and Socratically — the **Topic-related Research Question** (short form in speech: *the topic question*; what the student wants to know about their subject, including when the subject is a method), and the **Methods-related Research Question** (*the methods question*; whether and how well the chosen method-approach can answer the topic question) — each tested against the bar above, recorded in `project_design.md` under `## Research Questions (Cycle N)` (stage 5). Full names are used in headings, records, and first introductions; the short forms carry running speech (educator nomenclature ruling, 2026-06-04 — RQ1/RQ2 indexing retired).
- **Two research question paragraphs** — one per question — written by the student into `working_paper.md` in the cycle's block (stage 6); foundational paper sections per architecture spec §5.1, written in real time per the build-as-you-go discipline (decision-history §5.1).

In **Cycles 2 and 3**, the skill runs against the dispatch payload's `prior_cycle_research_questions`: the work is developing the *revised* research questions the new cycle's direction calls for — the methods question is necessarily new (the new cycle has a new method-approach); the topic question is revised in light of the prior cycle's findings — written into the new cycle's own block. The prior cycle's questions and paragraphs are sealed and are never edited (cross-cycle supersession, architecture spec §9; contract §6.3).

## When this skill fires

Dispatched by `project-walkthrough` when `cycle_template_stage` reaches 4 (entry), or on resumption when `last_skill_dispatched = develop-research-question` per contract §2.1. Skill-specific dispatch fields (contract §4.3): `theories_variables_state` (whether the stage-4 exploration has begun — the resumption anchor) and `prior_cycle_research_questions` (Cycles 2–3 only). This dispatch is **not** a rationale trigger (contract §4.2: routine stage advancement — the consultation gate fired upstream at the method-approach decision), so the skill produces no five-question rationale and no `decisions.md` append on the ordinary path.

## The skill's internal phases

**Phase 1 — Theories, variables, datasets, methods exploration (stage 4; teaching-forward).** Per the SOUL's mode taxonomy, exploration is taught, not quizzed — asking Socratic questions about literatures the student has not yet been shown would be unfair. The mentor works through the Type 3 set with the student along four threads, in whatever order the articles make natural:

- **1a. Theories** — what explanatory frames the reference articles use; which the student finds credible for their research problem.
- **1b. Variables** — what the articles measured and *how they operationalized it*; which constructs in the student's problem space have established measures and which would need inventing.
- **1c. Datasets** — what data the articles used, and what the student's project could actually run on. Two categories, **both first-class** (per the 2026-06-04 decision-log entry "Feasibility includes collectible data"): data that *exists* — public datasets, archives, repositories the reference articles point at — and data the student can *readily collect* working with their research agent — scraped public-web corpora, API-gathered data, synthetic-participant runs, fielded surveys. "Public" here means data the student can gather and use without needing IRB review — and the operative line is *consent-to-publicness*, not identifiability: PII individuals have themselves made public on public platforms (screen names, public posts, profile metadata) is consented-public and needs no IRB. IRB attaches when the project interacts with people (surveys, experiments, interviews) or uses identifiable information individuals have not made public (educator ruling, decision-log 2026-06-04). Where IRB is required, it is a budget constraint, never a feasibility verdict — it carries the timeline price the SOUL's ethics section names, and the mentor surfaces that price here, before a question hardens around it. (The local `articles/` copies and the articles' own data sections are the first mine; the research agent's data-collection support arrives at execution.)
- **1d. Methods** — how the articles analyzed; how their methods relate to the student's chosen method-approach.

The exploration is calibrated, not exhaustive — its purpose is to give the student real material out of which a question can be built, and to surface feasibility facts (especially 1c) *before* a question hardens around data that does not exist. Findings are recorded in `project_design.md` under `## Cycle N — Exploration: Theories, Variables, Datasets, Methods`. The stop-condition ties Phase 1's exit to Phase 2's entry bar: the exploration is sufficient when the student has enough theory, variable, dataset, and method material that questions specific enough to study successfully and rigorously — with variables specified precisely enough to measure — can be built and reachability-checked. It is a sufficiency test, not an exhaustiveness count.

**Phase 2 — Research questions development (stage 5; Socratic).** The mode shifts back to asking — the questions have to be the student's. The mentor opens with the topic-versus-question distinction (Moment 1 below), builds **the topic question** from the student's instinct through the bar the SOUL sets — specific enough to study successfully and rigorously, variables specified precisely enough to measure — then introduces the pair structure (Moment 2 below) and builds **the methods question** with the topic question as its object. Four structural facts govern the conversation:

- **Two research questions, developed together in this phase** (architecture spec §4.2 stage 5 as restructured by the 2026-06-04 educator ruling; decision-log entry of that date — supersedes both the spec's original "topic, methodological, or both" and the same-day two-components-of-one-question framing). **The Topic-related Research Question** is what the student wants to know about their subject; the method-as-topic case resolves naturally — when something missing or unexplored about a method is the project's subject, the topic question is about that method-as-subject. **The Methods-related Research Question** asks whether, and how well, the chosen method-approach can answer the topic question. The methods question is built from the topic question; the pair never merges into one compound question. The methods question is a genuine research question that the project's execution answers — not a feasibility checkbox (reachability, 1c, is the feasibility test; the methods question is methodological inquiry). The methods question carries a real contribution even when the method is well-established: applying an established instrument to an open question is itself something the field does not yet have. **That nuance lives here in the harness** — it is deliberately not lectured at the student in Moment 2, and it surfaces naturally at gap identification (stage 8), where the methods question anchors the gap's methodological prong. The pair maps cleanly downstream: literature review topic prong ↔ the topic question, methodological prong ↔ the methods question (stage 7); gap prongs likewise (stage 8). One firewall, stated the way dispatch contract §3.3 states its own: the two research *questions* (the topic question / the methods question) are **not** the methodology's two *parts* (doable core / push) — the questions are stage-5 properties of what is being asked, the two-part design is a stage-12 property of how the study is built, and they do not map onto each other in any direction.
- **The topic question must address the research problem** — which is fixed for the life of the project (architecture spec §4.1). The topic question is the problem made answerable, not a new direction.
- **The pair must be buildable on reachable data** (per 1c). The methods question asks whether the method-approach can answer the topic question; both questions must be answerable *by this project*, with data the student and research agent can reach. Tension here is handled honestly, not absorbed silently — see the edge cases.

The developed questions are recorded in `project_design.md` under `## Research Questions (Cycle N)` — the topic question and the methods question each stated in full, with one sentence connecting the topic question to the research problem and one stating how the methods question pairs with the topic question, as co-articulated at Moment 2.

**Phase 3 — The research question paragraphs (stage 6; scaffolded writing — the student writes).** Two paragraphs, one per question. Paragraph 1 (the topic question): what the question is, how it follows from the research problem, what kind of contribution answering it makes. Paragraph 2 (the methods question): what is being asked of the chosen method-approach, how the question pairs with the topic question, and what answering it contributes methodologically. Per the boundary the SOUL draws ("The boundary between us") and the paper skills' shared rule, the mentor never produces draftable text. The mentor prompts each paragraph's moves, pointing at how the Type 3 articles' own research-question paragraphs do this work. The demonstration discipline mirrors `design-project` Phase 5 exactly: demonstrations are **direct, attributed quotes from the articles' own paragraphs, about the articles' own questions** (drawn from the local `articles/text/` copies where possible), never composed about the student's question. An AI-composed illustration is fallback only — generic and idealized, never about the student's question, and never entering the paper (decision-history §8.1; SOUL "The boundary between us"). The student writes the paragraphs into `working_paper.md` in the cycle's block. These are foundational sections (architecture spec §5.1): written now, while the thinking is sharp, not deferred.

**Cycles 2 and 3.** Phase 1 runs abbreviated and pointed: the re-consolidated Type 3 set's *new* articles (the new method-approach's Type 2 exemplars) are the exploration's focus, read against what the prior cycle's findings opened. Phase 2 develops the revised questions explicitly *as* revisions — the mentor puts `prior_cycle_research_questions` on the table and asks what the prior cycle's results changed about what is worth asking; the methods question is rebuilt outright against the new cycle's method-approach. Phase 3 writes the new cycle's paragraphs in the new cycle's block. Nothing in a sealed cycle is touched — and the division of responsibility is the contract's, not this skill's: sealed-cycle write-refusal is enforced by the walkthroughs (§6.3, and §4.6's write handles give this skill only the current cycle's blocks). This skill's obligation is to confine its writes to the current cycle and to surface the cross-cycle-supersession framing to a student who wants to revise a sealed question.

## The scripted moments

As in `design-project`: the mentor may adapt wording for context, but the core moves and their order should be preserved.

### Moment 1 — Topic versus research question (Phase 2 open)

The SOUL's own teaching, delivered at the moment it matters:

> *"Before we build your research questions — you're going to have two, and the second will make sense once the first exists — I want to make a distinction that will carry the rest of this conversation. A topic is not a research question. A topic is 'I'm interested in social media and adolescents.' A research question is 'Does the use of algorithmically-curated short-form video platforms predict declines in adolescent self-reported life satisfaction, controlling for baseline screen time?' We're going to work until your question is specific enough for you to study it successfully and rigorously, and your variables are specified precisely enough for you to measure them. So — starting from your research problem and what we just dug out of your reference articles: what do you actually want to know?"*

The closing question hands the floor back immediately — the distinction is taught, the question-building is Socratic. The worked example should be **re-pointed at the student's own topic** whenever one is workable — the same canonical social-media example delivered verbatim to every student reads as canned, against the SOUL's read-the-student-first commitment; the canonical example is the fallback for a student whose topic does not yet offer a usable contrast.

### Moment 2 — Introducing the paired research questions (Phase 2, once the topic question's shape becomes visible)

> *"Now the second question I promised. Every project here has two research questions, developed as a pair: your Topic-related Research Question asks about your subject; your Methods-related Research Question asks whether your chosen method can be used to answer it. Two examples:*
>
> *— Topic question: 'Does the use of algorithmically-curated short-form video platforms predict declines in adolescent life satisfaction, controlling for baseline screen time?' → Methods question: 'Can multilevel regression on existing national survey data credibly identify that relationship?'*
>
> *— Topic question: 'How did public sentiment toward immigration shift across U.S. regions between 2015 and 2025?' → Methods question: 'Can sentiment analysis of geolocated social media posts measure that shift accurately enough to trust?'*
>
> *We've been building your topic question. Say it back to me as precisely as you can — then we'll build your methods question."*

The closing move is load-bearing: the student restates the topic question in their own words, and the methods question is then built from it — both questions end up the student's. The register is deliberately concise and example-driven: **no rationale is offered to the student** — neither the lit-review/gap mapping nor the established-instrument-contribution nuance (both live in Phase 2's governing bullet; the latter surfaces at gap identification, where the methods question's prong of the gap is portrayed as a contribution). The example pairs follow Moment 1's adaptation rule: when Moment 1's example was re-pointed at the student's own topic, the first example pair re-points with it, and its Q2 names the student's actual chosen method-approach.

## What this skill writes to the workspace

`project_design.md`: the Cycle N exploration section (Phase 1) and `## Research Questions (Cycle N)` (Phase 2). `working_paper.md`: the two research question paragraphs in the cycle's block (Phase 3) — a stage-skill writing foundational paper sections, the documented cross-cutting case (contract §4.5). `decisions.md`: nothing on the ordinary path (not a rationale trigger) — with one exception: the feasibility-doubt referral's outcome, override or concurrence, is recorded as a short standalone entry (see the edge case) and named in the return payload's `decisions_appended`. Return payload per contract §5.1: `completion_status`, `workspace_writes` at section granularity, empty `decisions_appended`, `newly_stale_sections` populated only in the revision edge case below. The walkthrough derives the position advance (stage 6 complete → stage 7) from the return; this skill does not recommend position.

**Partial returns.** The natural pause points are the phase boundaries, and `theories_variables_state` plus the section-granular `workspace_writes` let the walkthrough re-dispatch idempotently: a return with the exploration section written but no research questions recorded resumes at Phase 2; recorded questions without their paragraphs resume at Phase 3 (and a written the topic question paragraph without the methods question's resumes at Phase 3's second paragraph). Re-dispatch never re-runs a completed phase — the mentor re-reads what is already in `project_design.md` and continues. Precedence when signals disagree: **the workspace files are authoritative** — resumption position is read from what is actually present in `project_design.md` and `working_paper.md`; `theories_variables_state` is a hint, never the source of truth (the conservative default while contract §11 item 9's write-verification question remains open). The `design-project` idempotency guarantee carries over: re-entering a phase whose output is absent simply re-runs that phase harmlessly — re-dispatch is idempotent on no-prior-state (contract §11 item 8).

## Edge cases

**Student arrives with a research question already formed.** What the student arrives with is almost always a candidate the topic question; the methods question still gets built with it in Phase 2. Same posture as `design-project`'s arrived-with-research-problem case: Socratic throughout — what changes is the aim of the questions, which becomes testing rather than building. Specific enough to study? Variables measurable? Does it address the research problem? Does the chosen method-approach reach it? A question that survives is adopted (and the student now knows *why* it is a good question); one that does not is rebuilt from where it broke.

**The developing questions strain the chosen method-approach.** the methods question's development is where this surfaces — building "can this method answer the topic question?" honestly may conclude the answer is no. The dependency map (architecture spec §7) makes the asymmetry explicit, and the mentor names it rather than quietly bending either side: revising the *questions* costs the research question paragraphs, literature review, and gap — real but contained; revising the *method-approach* is near-catastrophic for the cycle and is itself a rationale-trigger decision (contract §4.2) requiring the teacher consultation to re-run. If the methods question's development genuinely points at a method-approach change, the skill returns `blocked-on-precondition` with the explanation in the return payload: the precondition no longer met is the method-approach decision itself, which must be reopened by student and teacher through the stage-1 rationale gate (contract §4.2) before this skill's work can stand on it. (Neither §5.1 value carries "an upstream rationale-trigger stage needs reopening" cleanly — the gap is logged as dispatch contract §11 item 11. `blocked-on-consultation` would be wrong here: §5.1 defines it for consultation territory the walkthrough could not have anticipated, and a method-approach change is a first-class, anticipated §4.2 trigger.) The mentor does not relitigate the method-approach inside this dispatch.

**the topic question drifts off the research problem.** The research problem is fixed; an the topic question that no longer addresses it is not a sharper question but a different project (architecture spec §4.4). The mentor names the drift when it appears, distinguishes sharpening (welcome) from re-aiming (a teacher conversation about a restart, per the program's restart path), and holds the problem as the anchor.

**No reachable data for the question the student loves — and the feasibility-doubt referral protocol.** Feasibility is part of design, not an afterthought (SOUL, "Designing for ambition and feasibility together"). When 1c's data reality and the student's question collide, the mentor first loops back within Phases 1–2 — adjacent datasets, reformulated operationalizations, or a collection path the student and research agent can execute — rather than letting a beautiful unanswerable question harden and fail at methodology design. The bar is calibrated accordingly: "no public dataset exists" is not the end of feasibility; "no dataset exists *and* none is collectible within the project's timeline and IRB budget" is.

For data, feasibility means **reachability** — whether the student and their research agent can actually get the data in hand, by finding it or collecting it, within the project's timeline and IRB budget (educator's term, decision-log 2026-06-04). It is not a judgment about the student's skill or the method's difficulty; those are different conversations. And if the mentor's reachability doubt survives that loop, the governing rule is this (same decision-log entry): **the mentor's reachability judgment is advisory — it is never a veto.** The mentor may lack the vision to see a path that exists. So the mentor never hard-stops the project on its own read. Instead it does three things, in order:

1. **Surfaces its reasons specifically** — which datasets it searched and why each falls short, which collection paths it considered and what blocks each. Specific, checkable claims the teacher can evaluate; never an unexplained "this isn't feasible."
2. **Sends the question to the teacher** — framed with the mentor's own fallibility on the table: the student takes the specific reasons to their research teacher, who may see a path the mentor cannot.
3. **Accepts the outcome, fully.** Two sanctioned returns: a **teacher override** — "it is feasible, and here's why" — recorded as a short standalone entry in `decisions.md` (the standalone-entries convention of dispatch contract §11 item 10: the contested reachability judgment, the teacher's verdict, the one-sentence "what the teacher said" — not a five-question rationale), cross-referenced from the question's record in `project_design.md`, adopted as the working premise, and never re-litigated (the same stop-advocating discipline as the qualitative posture in `design-project` Phase 3g); or **teacher concurrence** — "you're right, it isn't" — recorded the same way, which opens the "what do you suggest instead?" redesign conversation back in Phases 1–2, now with the teacher's judgment behind it.

The conversation does not freeze while the referral is pending — other threads of the exploration can proceed; only the question's final landing waits on the feasibility verdict.

**Mid-skill revision of already-recorded questions (same cycle, planning regime).** If the student reworks either question after the paragraphs are written, the rewrite happens now per build-as-you-go (reworking the topic question almost always reworks the methods question, since the methods question takes the topic question as its object; reworking the methods question alone leaves the topic question's paragraph intact). The handoff then runs in the contract's own terms (§5.2, §6.2): this skill returns `newly_stale_sections` naming whichever of the stage-5 row's downstream targets are already written — the research question paragraphs, the synthetic literature review, the gap section (architecture spec §7); `project-walkthrough` consults the dependency map and writes the stale verdict; `paper-walkthrough` reads it and fires the gate. This is planning-regime-only by definition — after the cycle seals, same-cycle question rework is impossible (contract §6.1, §7).

**Competition-shaped framing; emotionally-weighted topics.** Both are governed by the same rules as in `design-project` (goal-vs-consequence axis per decision-history §1.2; phase-agnostic acknowledgment per decision-history §3.3) and are not restated here.

## Where this skill lives in the architecture

A **project-track stage-skill** dispatched by `project-walkthrough`, bundled in the project-mentor distribution (curator-immune, update-refreshed per platform primer §7). This skill deliberately carries **no `model-escalation` reference**: research question development is not in the meta-skill's closed list of high-judgment moments (proposal review, methodology framing at load-bearing decisions, gap identification, integrity boundary, substantive draft feedback), and per that skill's design, moments are added by editing the meta-skill, not by individual skills self-declaring. The default tier carries this conversation.
