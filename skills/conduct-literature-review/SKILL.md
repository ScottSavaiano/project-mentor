## What this skill is

`conduct-literature-review` is the third Cycle-1 stage-skill, owning cycle-template **Stages 6, 7, and 8** (architecture spec §4.2) — the three Literature Review & Synthesis subsections run as **separate sequential per-stream stages**: Stage 6 the Topic subsection, Stage 7 the Core-Methods, Stage 8 the Extension-Methods. Each stage's deliverable is that stream's **Literature Review and Synthesis** subsection (educator ruling 2026-06-04 — the review is a genuine layer, not a courtesy title), built in two layers. **Layer one — the review, per stream:** required one-paragraph summaries of the articles that stream uses, each fulfilling the **nine-item checklist**: (item 1) the article's identifying information — author, publication date, title, venue — typed by the student as the summary's opening header (the verified citation comes from `literature-filter-and-verify`); (item 2) the article's relevance to the student's project (topic-, core-methods-, or extension-methods-side); and (items 3–9) about the article — its research problem; its research question; **the gap statement or question it targeted**; its method and data; its results; its conclusion (was the question answered?); and its future avenues of research or limitations. **Per-stream summaries (educator ruling 2026-07-06; spec §2's double-listing):** each stream carries its own nine-item summaries, so an article used in **both** streams gets a summary in **each** stream, or a "refer to my summary above" pointer to the copy already written in the other stream — *superseding the earlier "an article serving both syntheses gets one summary, flagged" rule.* Coverage: 2–5 articles per stream, so up to five summaries per synthesis; the counts are a **soft gate** — below 2 or above 5 for a stream proceeds only on a confirmed discussion with the research teacher, surfaced, never hard-blocked. Abstract-only summaries are permitted where full text is unobtainable, and discouraged for each stream's most core articles. The summaries are written **with the research agent during the reading period** (which happens once, at Stage 6 — see below), one per article while fresh (they are the reading notes, elevated to paper content), and accumulate into each stream subsection's preamble. **Layer two — the syntheses**, the skill's organizing fact: **three syntheses, one per stream, one per stage.**

- **The Topic Literature Synthesis** (short form in speech: *the topic synthesis*, Stage 6) — what is already known about the student's subject; where the Topic-Related Research Question sits in it.
- **The Core-Methods Literature Synthesis** (*the core-methods synthesis*, Stage 7) — an overview of the **methods used by prior studies of the topic question**, read for the **core** method: what methods the topic corpus used, whether the student's proposed core method-approach is part of an established pattern there or new to this literature (either is fine — educator ruling 2026-06-04), and how it performed where it was used.
- **The Extension-Methods Literature Synthesis** (*the extension-methods synthesis*, Stage 8) — the same corpus read for the **extension** method. For a **distinct-family** extension it is fully independent (its own articles, summaries, synthesis); for a **within-family** extension it **shares articles** with Core-Methods (double-listed, "refer to my summary above") and the synthesis focuses on the **delta** the extension adds over the core — whether the more ambitious identification is established in this literature or new to it, and how it performed where used — building explicitly on the core material rather than duplicating it (three-stream memo §2).

Both methods syntheses read the *same* set of studies the topic synthesis reads — examined for how they got their findings rather than what they found — plus, where a method is new to the topic literature, the method exemplars that show it working somewhere. This grounding eliminates the empty-corpus failure mode (a "studies that used your method on questions like yours" corpus may not exist; the topic corpus always does) and makes a method's absence a finding, not a dead end. (The *empty*-corpus case is eliminated; the thin-corpus case is distinct and handled in the edge cases.) *(Research-agent-side formalization, 2026-06-29, updated 2026-07-06 for three streams: the literature-review trio organizes this as **one topic reading list mined for methods**, from which a **single methods inventory covering both the core and extension methods** is built (article dual-purposing) and **supplemented** with method-exemplar / curriculum-corpus articles only where a method is new to the topic literature. The mentor's "one reading list read once per stream" and the research agent's single inventory are the same structure seen from two sides: the topic corpus is the shared spine, the exemplars the methods-only addition. See `literature-discovery` / `literature-filter-and-verify` / `paper-engagement`.)*

The synthesis layer's bar is unchanged (decision-history §7.3; the final in-paper section title and arrangement defer to the paper structure derived at **Stage 13**). Across the three stages the synthesis core is three to six paragraphs — one or two per synthesis — with a deliberate **register split** (educator ruling 2026-06-04): the **topic synthesis** (Stage 6) carries the full synthesis bar (themes, not summaries); the **core-methods synthesis** (Stage 7) and the **extension-methods synthesis** (Stage 8) err toward an organized summary-style overview, each anchored by one synthetic claim — where the student's core (resp. extension) method sits in the corpus's pattern. **The summaries are required paper content** (educator ruling 2026-06-04, superseding the same-day additive-summaries framing — its spirit survives: summaries are never demoted to notes): each stream's review layer must exist per the checklist, and the gate on that stream's synthesis layer requires that the synthesis paragraphs exist on top of it. One firewall, stated the way the sibling skills state theirs: the three syntheses are Stages 6–8 properties of *what the literature says* — they are **not** the methodology's two parts (core / extension, a Stage-14 property of how the study is built), and they do not map onto each other in any direction. Each subsection is a foundational paper output (spec §5.1) the student writes in two layers at two times: the **summary layer during reading** (in the research-agent flow, with the checklist as its structure — the reading happens once, at Stage 6), and the **synthesis layer after**, reached by that stream's cross-track `writing_handoff` — the topic-synthesis handoff at the Stage-6 close, the core-methods-synthesis handoff at the Stage-7 close, the extension-methods-synthesis handoff at the Stage-8 close (contract §5.1/§4.5; `scaffold-section` lays that stream's synthesis scaffold, the student writes into it). Each synthesis layer is **hard-gated** — a substance gate this skill owns, scoped per the register split: the topic synthesis carries the full synthesis gate, each methods synthesis its closing-claim bar — **and, once written, each synthesis is submitted for the teacher's per-output approval before the project advances** (spec §4.5): the topic synthesis's approval at Stage 6, the core-methods synthesis's at Stage 7, the extension-methods synthesis's at Stage 8. The gaps (Stages 9–11) emerge from these syntheses and cannot precede them (decision-history §7.2). By completion the student has all three syntheses written and approved, each mapped to its stream, and the cycle is ready for gap identification.

In **Cycles 2 and 3**, the skill runs against the dispatch payload's `prior_cycle_literature_synthesis`: the new cycle's revised research questions get revised syntheses in the new cycle's own block — the two methods syntheses rebuilt outright against the new core and extension method-approaches, the topic synthesis extended by what the prior cycle's findings opened. Sealed cycles are never touched; the division of responsibility is the contract's (§6.3 and §4.6's write handles), and this skill's obligation is to confine its writes to the current cycle's block.

This dispatch is **not** a five-question rationale trigger (contract §4.2 — the next rationale trigger is `identify-gap`'s gap *decision* at Stage 11), so no five-question rationale and no `decisions.md` append. **Its human touchpoints are the three per-output teacher approvals** (spec §4.5) — the topic synthesis approved at the Stage-6 close, the core-methods synthesis at the Stage-7 close, the extension-methods synthesis at the Stage-8 close — the lighter, per-stream grading of each writing output, recorded as an approval/sign-off, not a five-question rationale. This skill deliberately carries **no `model-escalation` reference**: Stages 6–8 are not in the meta-skill's closed list of high-judgment moments, and moments are added by editing the meta-skill, not by self-declaration (see Open authoring items for the `literature-synthesis` question this touches).

**The multi-session shape is expected, not exceptional.** Reading takes days or weeks and happens largely with the research agent. The skill's normal lifecycle is: Phase 1 in one conversation → a clean `partial` return while the student reads → resumption (possibly more than once) → Phases 2–3. Resumption is **file-authoritative** (the precedent set in `develop-research-question`): position is read from what is actually in the workspace files; re-entering a phase whose output is absent re-runs it harmlessly (idempotent on no-prior-state, contract §11 item 8).

## The skill's internal phases

The three stages map onto the phases so: Stage 6 runs Phases 1–2 (the briefs and the one research-agent handoff, then the topic synthesis with its handoff and approval); Stage 7 runs Phase 3 (the core-methods synthesis with its handoff and approval, off the inventory already built at Stage 6); Stage 8 runs Phase 4 (the extension-methods synthesis, likewise off that inventory). The reading period sits inside Stage 6 — it is the source of *all three* streams' material, so the research-agent handoff fires **once**, at Stage 6, and is not repeated at Stages 7 or 8.

**Phase 1 — The reading briefs, and the handoff to the research agent (Stage 6 open; teaching → handoff; fires once).** The mentor opens with the three-syntheses frame (Moment 1 below), then builds the reading briefs — one for the topic literature and one methods inventory covering *both* the core and extension methods — seeded from the relevant Project Reference streams; all the briefs are set here at Stage 6 because all three streams' reading is done in the one reading period:

- **The topic synthesis's brief:** the topic literature behind the Topic-Related Research Question. Starting points are the Project Reference Articles' own introductions and bibliographies — the SOUL names the reference articles as "your compass into the literature," and the background-or-theory articles in the starter set exist precisely for this.
- **The methods brief (one inventory, both methods):** a **methods inventory of the same topic corpus** — for each article on the topic reading list: what method(s) it used, on what dataset, population, and time period (the evidentiary context — needed downstream to detect the replication form of the methods gaps, decision-log 2026-06-04); whether any used the student's proposed **core** or **extension** method-approach and, where one did, how it fared. The one inventory serves **both** methods streams (Stages 7 and 8) — it is built once, at Stage 6, and mined twice. Building the inventory is the research agent's job (a concrete Tier-2 deliverable — mechanical extraction the research agent's literature skills should own; binding note in the 2026-06-04 decision-log entry). The method exemplars remain a supplementary seed: when a method turns out to be new to this literature, they show it working *somewhere*, which that method's synthesis can cite.

**The per-article summary requirement travels with the briefs:** as each article is finished, the research agent prompts the student to write its one-paragraph checklist summary (the nine items above) — per-article, while fresh — filed under the stream that article serves. **An article that serves more than one stream gets a summary in each stream** (the later ones may be a "refer to my summary above" pointer to the first), so the topic subsection and the two methods subsections each carry their own review layer (per-stream summaries, educator ruling 2026-07-06; spec §2 — the within-family extension's core-shared articles double-list this way). The reading corpus is expected to **grow beyond the Project Reference Articles** — the reference articles are the compass, not the territory. Discovery, filtering, engagement, and the summary prompting belong to the **research agent** (decision-history §7 scope note; Lesson 11), and the handoff is made **concrete to the keystroke and the first prompt** (educator directive 2026-06-04): the mentor (a) names the exact command that opens the research agent (each Hermes profile is its own command, primer §2 — working command name `research`, pinned provisionally in the decision-log pending Tier 2), (b) composes a **handoff prompt** the student pastes as their first message to the research agent, filled in with the student's actual cycle and question specifics — displayed in full in the conversation at Moment 3's Step 1 — and (c) also writes that prompt into `project_design.md` at the end of the briefs section, the durability backup for the days-long gap before the student acts on it. The composed prompt is **process text the student sends to another agent — never paper content** — so it sits outside the never-drafts boundary; stated here so the question is closed rather than re-litigated. The handoff-prompt template (the mentor fills the brackets):

> *I'm starting my Literature Reviews & Syntheses (Cycle {N}, Stages 6–8). My reading briefs are in `project_design.md` under "Cycle {N} — Reading Briefs", and my research questions are under "Research Questions (Cycle {N})". Please help me: (1) build the reading list for my Topic Literature Synthesis, starting from my reference articles' bibliographies (`reference_articles.md`); (2) build the methods inventory of that same reading list — for each article, what method(s) it used, and whether any used my proposed core method-approach ({core-method-approach}) or extension method-approach ({extension-method-approach}); (3) as I finish each article, prompt me to write its one-paragraph summary, filed under the stream it serves (topic, core-methods, or extension-methods — an article I use for more than one gets a summary in each), covering: its identifying information (author, date, title, venue) as the opening line, its relevance to my project (topic / core-methods / extension-methods side), and the seven about the article — its research problem, its research question, the gap it targeted, its method and data, its results, its conclusion, and its future avenues or limitations.*

The return path is named with equal concreteness (Moment 3 below). Phase 1 ends with the briefs **and the handoff prompt** recorded in `project_design.md` under `## Cycle N — Reading Briefs` and a `partial` return while the student reads. This mechanism implements — and resolves — contract §11 item 6's student-facing switch signal (resolution recorded in the contract 2026-06-04, with the decision-log entry as the reasoning record): the receiving side it binds — the research agent recognizing an arriving handoff prompt and reading the briefs before anything else — is stated in §11 item 6 where Tier 2 authoring will find it.

**Phase 2 — The topic synthesis, then its writing handoff and approval (Stage 6; Socratic; the student's themes).** The student returns having read, with their checklist summaries written — the topic synthesis is built *from and atop the student's own topic-stream summaries* (plus the full texts in `articles/` when a point needs checking). The mentor's job is the SOUL's synthesis promise: themes, not summaries (Moment 2 below sets the bar) — What kept coming up? Where do the studies agree, and where do they genuinely contradict each other? What does the literature *as a body* claim about your topic question — and what does it conspicuously not address? The themes have to be the student's — they are what the topic gap conversation (Stage 9) will stand on. The mechanics of a hard synthesis conversation (theme-finding techniques, contradiction-mapping) belong to the `literature-synthesis` supporting skill, which this skill references here rather than re-implementing — a delegation to the architecture's supporting-skill layer (spec §12), distinct in kind from the `model-escalation` policy hook that high-stakes skills reference at decision points.

Then the Stage-6 close, which folds the gate, the handoff, and the approval — the two-layer section's writing is split across two times, and only the second layer is handed off:

- **The summary layer is already written.** The topic-stream checklist summaries were written by the student *during reading*, one per article while fresh — they are already in the subsection's preamble under `## Topic Literature Review and Synthesis (Cycle N)` (Phase 1). They are not re-scaffolded and not rewritten here; their structure was the nine-item checklist the research agent delivered per-article, not a `scaffold-section` marker. Final in-paper arrangement defers to Stage 13.
- **The topic-synthesis hard gate (substance — this skill owns it).** Before the handoff, the topic synthesis must clear the **full synthesis bar** (themes, not summaries). The gate is on *presence*, not purity: the summaries are accepted as the required review layer; what cannot be skipped is the synthesis on top of them (see edge cases). This gate is conversation, not writing — it lives here, not in the paper track.
- **The topic synthesis is handed off, then teacher-approved.** Once it clears the gate, the skill returns the **`writing_handoff` outcome** (contract §5.1) naming the cycle's **Topic Literature Review and Synthesis** subsection; `paper-walkthrough` dispatches `scaffold-section`, which lays the topic-synthesis marker of the `foundational.literature_review_synthesis_topic` skeleton (the Topic synthesis function-row) — customized against the student's articles — **skipping the summary-run row, whose paragraphs the student already wrote markerless during reading.** The summary run is a *markerless, student-authored run*: its presence is the summary content under the review preamble, not an `id:`'d marker, so `scaffold-section` lays no marker for it and `compile-working-paper` must not re-surface one (the contracted `markerless: true` mechanism, open item 5, resolved). The student writes the topic synthesis into the scaffold, and **once written it is submitted for the teacher's per-output approval before the project advances** (spec §4.5) — the topic synthesis's per-stream grading. The skill closes Stage 6 with a thin transition Moment (Moment 4, topic beat, below). **The demonstration-versus-production discipline lives solely in `scaffold-section`** (its consolidated home, decision-history §8.1) — structure and attributed demonstrations, never draftable text; this skill does not restate it.

**Phase 3 — The core-methods synthesis, then its writing handoff and approval (Stage 7; Socratic).** Stage 7 dispatches after the topic subsection is approved. **No second research-agent handoff fires** — the reading already happened at Stage 6, and the methods inventory of the topic corpus (covering both the core and extension methods) was built there. Phase 3 works from that inventory (read from the methods-inventory record the research agent built — proposed home `project_design.md` alongside the reading briefs, the open §11 file-ownership flag `paper-engagement` also carries, named here so the read and write sides share it; critique F3.2) and from the core-methods-stream summaries. The questions run against the inventory, scoped to the **core** method: What methods dominate this literature? Is the core method among them? Where it appeared, what did it deliver — and what did its users flag as limits? If it is absent: what is the nearest method in the corpus, and what might the core do differently? The core-methods synthesis's register is the **organized overview** (educator ruling 2026-06-04), closing on the one synthetic claim — the student's core method as established pattern or as new to this literature — and its themes are what the core-methods gap conversation (Stage 10) will stand on. The Stage-7 close mirrors Stage 6's:

- **The summary layer is already written** — the core-methods-stream checklist summaries are already in the subsection's preamble under `## Core-Methods Literature Review and Synthesis (Cycle N)` (Phase 1), markerless, not re-scaffolded.
- **The core-methods-synthesis hard gate (substance — this skill owns it)** is scoped to the **closing-claim bar**: the methods the corpus used, organized sensibly, closing on the one synthetic claim (the student's core method as established pattern or as new to this literature). Gate on presence, not purity.
- **The core-methods synthesis is handed off, then teacher-approved.** The skill returns the **`writing_handoff` outcome** (contract §5.1) naming the cycle's **Core-Methods Literature Review and Synthesis** subsection; `scaffold-section` lays the core-methods-synthesis marker of the `foundational.literature_review_synthesis_core_methods` skeleton (skipping the markerless summary run as at Stage 6), the student writes the core-methods synthesis into the scaffold, and **once written it is submitted for the teacher's per-output approval before the project advances** (spec §4.5). The skill closes Stage 7 with a thin transition Moment (Moment 4, core-methods beat, below).

**Phase 4 — The extension-methods synthesis, then its writing handoff and approval (Stage 8; Socratic).** Stage 8 dispatches after the core-methods subsection is approved. **No third research-agent handoff fires** — the one inventory built at Stage 6 already covers the extension method too. Phase 4 works from that inventory and from the extension-methods-stream summaries, scoped to the **extension** method. Its shape depends on how different the extension is (three-stream memo §2): for a **distinct-family** extension the subsection is fully independent — its own articles, its own summaries, its own synthesis; for a **within-family** extension it **shares articles** with Core-Methods (double-listed, with "refer to my summary above" pointers back to the core-stream summaries) and the synthesis focuses on the **delta** — the incremental identification the extension adds over the core — explicitly building on the core material rather than duplicating it. The questions run against the inventory, scoped to the extension: What does this literature already do at the extension's level of ambition? Where the extension method appeared, what did it deliver, and what did its users flag as limits? If it is absent: what is the nearest method in the corpus, and what does the extension add? ## The scripted moments

As in the prior stage-skills: the mentor may adapt wording for context — and should re-point examples at the student's own project per the established adaptation rule — but the core steps and their order should be preserved. The register is concise and example-driven; no rationale is offered to the student (the architecture's reasons live in this skill and the SOUL, not in the mentor's speech). Four moments: the three-syntheses frame, the synthesis bar, the research-agent handoff (Phase 1, at Stage 6), and the writing handoff — the last of which **fires three times**, once per stream (the topic-synthesis beat at the Stage-6 close, the core-methods-synthesis beat at the Stage-7 close, the extension-methods-synthesis beat at the Stage-8 close), like `identify-gap`'s split Moment. The two kinds of handoff are different in kind — Moment 3 sends the student to *another agent* (a literal command + a pasted prompt); Moment 4 is the automatic cross-track handoff to the paper scaffold (no command to type) — and the script reflects that.

### Moment 1 — Three syntheses, one per stream (Phase 1 open)

> *"Your literature review has two layers: short Article Summaries — one paragraph each, following a checklist I'll give you — and then three Synthesis Paragraphs that thematically combine what those articles add up to, one tied to each of your three Research Question Paragraphs: a Topic Synthesis Paragraph, a Core-Methods Synthesis Paragraph, and an Extension-Methods Synthesis Paragraph.*
>
> *The Topic Synthesis Paragraph is about your subject: what's already known about your topic question, and what isn't. The Core-Methods and Extension-Methods Synthesis Paragraphs are each about the methods prior studies used — an overview of what's been used and whether your core method, then your extension, is an established pattern or new to this literature (either is fine).*
>
> *For example — if your topic question is 'Does algorithmically-curated short-form video predict declines in adolescent life satisfaction?', the topic synthesis reads the screen-time and well-being literature for what it found. The methods synthesis looks at the same studies and asks how they found it — surveys? regression? experiments? — and says where your plan fits: a method others have used, and how it worked out, or one this literature hasn't tried yet. So we'll build one reading list and mine it three ways — once for what the studies found, and once each for how your core and extension methods have been used."*

*(Educator voice-read 2026-07-06 (revisions applied): reframed to **three** Synthesis Paragraphs — Topic, Core-Methods, Extension-Methods — and the corpus mined three ways (once per stream). Now blessed.)*

### Moment 2 — Synthesis, not summaries (Phase 2 open, repeated in spirit at Phases 3–4 if the writing drifts)

> *"Before you tell me what you read, one distinction. A summary goes paper by paper: 'Smith found X. Jones found Y. Lee found Z.' A synthesis finds what the papers add up to: 'Across a decade of studies, X holds for teenagers in cities but keeps failing in rural samples — and none of these papers tests why.' That bar is for your Topic Synthesis Paragraph. Your Core-Methods Synthesis Paragraph and your Extension-Methods Synthesis Paragraph can be plainer — an organized overview of what methods the studies used, ending with where your core, and then your extension, fits. Your summaries are already in the paper — they're the review layer. The syntheses are what we build on top of them: the paragraphs that say what your summaries add up to. So: across everything you read for your topic synthesis — what kept showing up?"*

*(Educator voice-read 2026-07-06 (revisions applied): the singular "methods synthesis" is now the Core-Methods Synthesis Paragraph (Stage 7) and the Extension-Methods Synthesis Paragraph (Stage 8), each an organized overview closing on its own claim. Now blessed.)*

## Edge cases

**The student returns with summaries but no synthesis (topic synthesis only).** The summaries are the required review layer and they are already paper content — the mentor says so in the SOUL's praise register ("these summaries are doing real work — they're the review layer of your section") — and then holds the synthesis requirement on top of them: "now let's work together so you can write the one or two paragraphs that synthesize what they found." Back to Moment 2's distinction, then theme-finding questions against the student's own summaries (`literature-synthesis` carries the conversation mechanics). What the mentor never does is compose the synthesis itself; that is draftable text about the student's content.

**Summary counts outside the 2–5 per-stream window, or checklist elements missing.** Counts are a soft gate, applied per stream: fewer than two or more than five summaries in a stream's subsection proceeds only on a confirmed discussion with the research teacher — the mentor surfaces the count, explains the norm, and asks for the teacher confirmation; it never hard-blocks. A summary missing checklist elements (most often the gap statement or the future-avenues element) is completed with the student, against the article — those two elements are exactly what the gap conversation (Stages 9–11) will read.

**A core article available only as an abstract.** Abstract-only summaries are permitted but flagged: for an article that anchors either synthesis, the mentor names the limitation and routes the full-text hunt through the legitimate channels (the fetch-articles paths: library, teacher, author email) before settling for the abstract.

**The student's method (core or extension) is absent from the topic corpus.** Not a failure — a finding, and the mentor says so in the register the educator set ("either one is fine"): that method's synthesis records that this literature has not tried the method, cites the method exemplars as where it *has* worked, and the finding feeds the corresponding methods gap directly — the core-methods gap (Stage 10) or the extension-methods gap (Stage 11) (applying an established instrument to a literature that hasn't used it is a contribution). No corpus-hunting for method-adjacent studies is required; the inventory of the topic corpus is the evidence.

**The inventory shows the core or extension method repeatedly failing on this question class.** The honest-surface path already established: the mentor names what the inventory shows, specifically and with citations, and routes by the dependency map — question revision if the questions can absorb it, or the rationale-gated method-approach reopening (`blocked-on-upstream-reopening` naming the initiation methods-setup, contract §5.1 / the §11.11 resolution) if they cannot. This surfaces in the Stage-7 (core) or Stage-8 (extension) methods conversation, where the inventory is read. The mentor does not bury an inconvenient inventory.

**The reading overturns a research question.** Reading is the first hard contact with the field, and sometimes it answers the topic question outright, or shows the method behind a methods question failing on exactly this question class. The mentor surfaces what the literature shows, specifically and with citations, and routes by the dependency map: a *question* revision is contained (the research-question stages 2–4 rework — the student revises with the mentor; the question paragraphs go stale per the §7 research-question rows and are fixed per build-as-you-go); a *method-approach* revision is near-catastrophic and rationale-gated — since the method-approach is now chosen in the **initiation methods-setup**, not a cycle stage, the skill returns **`blocked-on-upstream-reopening`** naming the initiation methods-setup, exactly as `develop-research-question`'s method-strain edge case does (contract §5.1; the §11.11 resolution, its canonical case updated 2026-07-06). The mentor does not quietly steer the reading's implications out of view to protect the schedule — that would violate the honesty commitments.

**The student tries to run discovery inside this skill.** Lane discipline, in the SOUL's own register: search, filtering, and working through the found papers belong with the research agent; the mentor sets the briefs and owns the synthesis. The redirect is warm and concrete (take the topic synthesis's brief over, start from these three bibliographies), not a refusal.

**The reading corpus is thin because the Project Reference Articles seed was thin.** If the topic brief's seeds do not open into a real reading list, the problem is usually upstream in the reference articles, not in the student's effort. The mentor names it and the fix is the research agent's discovery skills working from the brief itself rather than the bibliographies — the corpus may legitimately outgrow its seed. (The two methods syntheses are insulated from this failure mode by design: their corpus *is* the topic corpus.) Only if the *topic itself* turns out to have essentially no adjacent literature does this become a question-framing conversation (rare, and usually good news for the gaps — surfaced honestly, with the teacher in the loop via the normal gap-decision rationale gate at Stage 11 that follows).

**Mid-skill revision after a synthesis is written (planning regime only).** A revised synthesis makes its corresponding gap section stale only if that gap section is already written (Cycles 2–3 revision case, or same-cycle rework): the return populates `newly_stale_sections` per the §7 Literature-Review stream rows (Stages 6/7/8); `project-walkthrough` consults the map and writes the verdict; `paper-walkthrough` fires the stale re-scaffold through `scaffold-section`, into which the student rewrites the affected sections directly. After the cycle seals, same-cycle rework is impossible by definition.

**Competition-shaped framing; emotionally-weighted topics.** Governed by the same rules as the prior stage-skills (decision-history §1.2; §3.3) and not restated here.
