## What this skill is

`conduct-literature-review` is the third Cycle-1 stage-skill, owning cycle-template stage 7 (architecture spec §4.2). The deliverable is the **Literature Review and Synthesis** section (educator ruling 2026-06-04 — the review is a genuine layer, not a courtesy title), built in two layers. **Layer one — the review:** required one-paragraph summaries of each included article, each fulfilling the seven-element checklist: the article's research problem; its research question; **the gap statement or question it targeted**; its method and data; its results; its conclusion (was the question answered?); and its future avenues of research or limitations. Coverage: 2–5 articles per synthesis, overlap allowed (an article serving both syntheses gets one summary, flagged), so 2–10 distinct summaries; the counts are a **soft gate** — below 2 or above 10 proceeds only on a confirmed discussion with the research teacher, surfaced, never hard-blocked. Abstract-only summaries are permitted where full text is unobtainable, and discouraged for each review's most core articles. The summaries are written **with the research agent during the reading period**, one per article while fresh (they are the reading notes, elevated to paper content), and accumulate into the section's preamble. **Layer two — the syntheses**, the skill's organizing fact: **two syntheses, one per research question.**

- **The Topic-related Literature Synthesis** (short form in speech: *the topic synthesis*) — what is already known about the student's subject; where the Topic-related Research Question sits in it.
- **The Methods-related Literature Synthesis** (*the methods synthesis*) — an overview of the **methods used by prior studies of the topic question**: what methods the topic corpus used, whether the student's proposed method-approach is part of an established pattern there or new to this literature (either is fine — educator ruling 2026-06-04), and how it performed where it was used. The corpus is the *same* set of studies the topic synthesis reads — examined for how they got their findings rather than what they found. This grounding eliminates the empty-corpus failure mode (a "studies that used your method on questions like yours" corpus may not exist; the topic corpus always does) and makes the method's absence a finding, not a dead end. (The *empty*-corpus case is eliminated; the thin-corpus case is distinct and handled in the edge cases.)

The synthesis layer's bar is unchanged (decision-history §7.3; the final in-paper section title and arrangement defer to the paper structure derived at stage 11). The synthesis core is two to four paragraphs — one or two per synthesis — with a deliberate **register split** (educator ruling 2026-06-04): the topic synthesis carries the full synthesis bar (themes, not summaries); the methods synthesis errs toward an organized summary-style overview, anchored by one synthetic claim — where the student's method sits in the corpus's pattern. **The summaries are required paper content** (educator ruling 2026-06-04, superseding the same-day additive-summaries framing — its spirit survives: summaries are never demoted to notes): the review layer must exist per the checklist, and the gate on the synthesis layer requires that the synthesis paragraphs exist on top of it. One firewall, stated the way the sibling skills state theirs: the two syntheses are stage-7 properties of *what the literature says* — they are **not** the methodology's two parts (doable core / push, a stage-12 property of how the study is built), and they do not map onto each other in any direction. The deliverable is a foundational section (spec §5.1) the student writes in two layers at two times: the **summary layer during reading** (in the research-agent flow, with the checklist as its structure — see Phase 1), and the **synthesis layer after**, reached by the cross-track `writing_handoff` this skill returns at Phase 3 (contract §5.1/§4.5; `scaffold-section` lays the synthesis scaffold, the student writes into it). The synthesis layer is **hard-gated** — a substance gate this skill owns, scoped per the register split: the topic synthesis carries the synthesis gate, the methods synthesis its closing-claim bar (Phase 3) — and the gaps (stage 8) emerge from this synthesis and cannot precede it (decision-history §7.2). By completion the student has both syntheses written, each mapped to its research question, and the cycle is ready for gap identification.

In **Cycles 2 and 3**, the skill runs against the dispatch payload's `prior_cycle_literature_synthesis`: the new cycle's revised research questions get a revised synthesis in the new cycle's own block — the methods synthesis rebuilt outright against the new method-approach, the topic synthesis extended by what the prior cycle's findings opened. Sealed cycles are never touched; the division of responsibility is the contract's (§6.3 and §4.6's write handles), and this skill's obligation is to confine its writes to the current cycle's block.

## The skill's internal phases

**Phase 1 — The two reading briefs, and the handoff to the research agent (teaching → handoff).** The mentor opens with the two-syntheses frame (Moment 1 below), then builds a reading brief *per synthesis*, seeded from the Project Reference Articles:

- **The topic synthesis's brief:** the topic literature behind the Topic-related Research Question. Starting points are the Project Reference Articles' own introductions and bibliographies — the SOUL names the reference articles as "your compass into the literature," and the background-or-theory articles in the starter set exist precisely for this.
- **The methods synthesis's brief:** a **methods inventory of the same topic corpus** — for each article on the topic reading list: what method(s) it used, on what dataset, population, and time period (the evidentiary context — needed downstream to detect the replication form of the Methods-related Gap, decision-log 2026-06-04); whether any used the student's proposed method-approach and, where one did, how it fared. Building the inventory is the research agent's job (a concrete Tier-2 deliverable — mechanical extraction the research agent's literature skills should own; binding note in the 2026-06-04 decision-log entry). The method exemplars remain a supplementary seed: when the method turns out to be new to this literature, they show it working *somewhere*, which the methods synthesis can cite.

**The per-article summary requirement travels with the briefs:** as each article is finished, the research agent prompts the student to write its one-paragraph checklist summary (the seven elements above) — per-article, while fresh. The reading corpus is expected to **grow beyond the Project Reference Articles** — the reference articles are the compass, not the territory. Discovery, filtering, engagement, and the summary prompting belong to the **research agent** (decision-history §7 scope note; Lesson 11), and the handoff is made **concrete to the keystroke and the first prompt** (educator directive 2026-06-04): the mentor (a) names the exact command that opens the research agent (each Hermes profile is its own command, primer §2 — working command name `research`, pinned provisionally in the decision-log pending Tier 2), (b) composes a **handoff prompt** the student pastes as their first message to the research agent, filled in with the student's actual cycle and question specifics — displayed in full in the conversation at Moment 3's Step 1 — and (c) also writes that prompt into `project_design.md` at the end of the briefs section, the durability backup for the days-long gap before the student acts on it. The composed prompt is **process text the student sends to another agent — never paper content** — so it sits outside the never-drafts boundary; stated here so the question is closed rather than re-litigated. The handoff-prompt template (the mentor fills the brackets):

> *I'm starting my Literature Synthesis (Cycle {N}, stage 7). My reading briefs are in `project_design.md` under "Cycle {N} — Reading Briefs", and my research questions are under "Research Questions (Cycle {N})". Please help me: (1) build the reading list for my Topic-related Literature Synthesis, starting from my reference articles' bibliographies (`reference_articles.md`); (2) build the methods inventory of that same reading list — for each article, what method(s) it used, and whether any used my proposed method-approach ({method-approach}); (3) as I finish each article, prompt me to write its one-paragraph summary covering: the article's research problem, its research question, the gap it targeted, its method and data, its results, its conclusion, and its future avenues or limitations.*

The return path is named with equal concreteness (Moment 3 below). Phase 1 ends with the briefs **and the handoff prompt** recorded in `project_design.md` under `## Cycle N — Reading Briefs` and a `partial` return while the student reads. This mechanism implements — and resolves — contract §11 item 6's student-facing switch signal (resolution recorded in the contract 2026-06-04, with the decision-log entry as the reasoning record): the receiving side it binds — the research agent recognizing an arriving handoff prompt and reading the briefs before anything else — is stated in §11 item 6 where Tier 2 authoring will find it.

**Phase 2 — Synthesis, one at a time (Socratic; the student's themes).** The student returns having read, with their checklist summaries written — the syntheses are built *from and atop the student's own summaries* (plus the full texts in `articles/` when a point needs checking). For the **topic synthesis**, the mentor's job is the SOUL's synthesis promise: themes, not summaries (Moment 2 below sets the bar) — What kept coming up? Where do the studies agree, and where do they genuinely contradict each other? What does the literature *as a body* claim about your question — and what does it conspicuously not address? For the **methods synthesis**, the questions run against the inventory: What methods dominate this literature? Is yours among them? Where it appeared, what did it deliver — and what did its users flag as limits? If it is absent: what is the nearest method in the corpus, and what might yours do differently? The themes have to be the student's — they are what the gap conversation (stage 8) will stand on. The mechanics of a hard synthesis conversation (theme-finding techniques, contradiction-mapping) belong to the `literature-synthesis` supporting skill, which this skill references at Phase 2 rather than re-implementing — a delegation to the architecture's supporting-skill layer (spec §12), distinct in kind from the `model-escalation` policy hook that high-stakes skills reference at decision points.

**Phase 3 — The synthesis gate, then the writing handoff (the two-layer section).** This section's writing is split across two times, and only the second layer is handed off here:

- **The summary layer is already written.** The checklist summaries were written by the student *during reading*, in the research-agent flow, one per article while fresh — they are already in the section's preamble under `## Literature Review and Synthesis (Cycle N)` (Phase 1). They are not re-scaffolded and not rewritten here; their structure was the seven-element checklist the research agent delivered per-article, not a `scaffold-section` marker. Final in-paper arrangement defers to stage 11.
- **The synthesis hard gate (substance — this skill owns it).** Before the handoff, the synthesis substance must clear the gate, scoped per the register split: the **topic synthesis** carries the full synthesis bar (themes, not summaries), the **methods synthesis** its closing-claim bar (the methods the corpus used, organized sensibly, closing on the one synthetic claim — the student's method as established pattern or as new to this literature). The gate is on *presence*, not purity: the summaries are accepted as the required review layer; what cannot be skipped is the synthesis on top of them (see edge cases). This gate is conversation, not writing — it lives here, not in the paper track.
- **The synthesis layer is handed off.** Once the synthesis substance clears the gate, the skill returns the **`writing_handoff` outcome** (contract §5.1) naming the cycle's Literature Review and Synthesis section; `paper-walkthrough` dispatches `scaffold-section`, which lays the synthesis markers of the `foundational.literature_review_synthesis` skeleton (the Topic-related and Methods-related synthesis function-rows) — customized against the student's articles — **skipping the summary-run row, whose paragraphs the student already wrote markerless during reading.** The summary run is a *markerless, student-authored run*: its presence is the summary content under the review preamble, not an `id:`'d marker, so `scaffold-section` lays no marker for it and `compile-working-paper` must not re-surface one. *(The summary-run row is `required: true` in `section-scaffolds.yaml`, and `compile-working-paper`'s default on a dropped `required` item is to re-surface its marker — which this design forbids for the summary run. The detection that distinguishes a present markerless run from a genuinely dropped one, without an `id:` anchor, is a `compile-working-paper`/contract question — open item 5 below, flagged for the contract/compile docket, not solved here.)* The student writes the synthesis paragraphs into the scaffold. The skill closes with a thin transition Moment (Moment 4 below). **The demonstration-versus-production discipline lives solely in `scaffold-section`** (its consolidated home, decision-history §8.1) — structure and attributed demonstrations, never draftable text; this skill does not restate it.

## The scripted moments

As in the prior stage-skills: the mentor may adapt wording for context — and should re-point examples at the student's own project per the established adaptation rule — but the core steps and their order should be preserved. The register is concise and example-driven; no rationale is offered to the student (the architecture's reasons live in this skill and the SOUL, not in the mentor's speech). Four moments: the two-syntheses frame, the synthesis bar, the research-agent handoff (Phase 1), and the writing handoff (Phase 3). The two handoffs are different in kind — Moment 3 sends the student to *another agent* (a literal command + a pasted prompt); Moment 4 is the automatic cross-track handoff to the paper scaffold (no command to type) — and the script reflects that.

### Moment 1 — Two syntheses, one per question (Phase 1 open)

> *"Your literature review has two layers: short summaries of each article you use — one paragraph each, following a checklist I'll give you — and then two syntheses that say what those articles add up to, one for each of your research questions.*
>
> *The Topic-related Literature Synthesis is about your subject: what's already known about your topic question, and what isn't. The Methods-related Literature Synthesis is about the methods used by prior studies of your topic question: an overview of what methods have been used, and whether your proposed method is part of an established pattern or is new to this literature — either one is fine.*
>
> *For example — if your topic question is 'Does algorithmically-curated short-form video predict declines in adolescent life satisfaction?', the topic synthesis reads the screen-time and well-being literature for what it found. The methods synthesis looks at the same studies and asks how they found it — surveys? regression? experiments? — and says where your plan fits: a method others have used, and how it worked out, or one this literature hasn't tried yet. So we'll build one reading list and read it twice — once for what the studies found, once for how they found it."*

### Moment 2 — Synthesis, not summaries (Phase 2 open, repeated in spirit at Phase 3 if the writing drifts)

> *"Before you tell me what you read, one distinction. A summary goes paper by paper: 'Smith found X. Jones found Y. Lee found Z.' A synthesis finds what the papers add up to: 'Across a decade of studies, X holds for teenagers in cities but keeps failing in rural samples — and none of these papers tests why.' That bar is for your topic synthesis. Your methods synthesis can be plainer — an organized overview of what methods the studies used, ending with where yours fits. Your summaries are already in the paper — they're the review layer. The syntheses are what we build on top of them: the paragraphs that say what your summaries add up to. So: across everything you read for your topic synthesis — what kept showing up?"*

### Moment 3 — The handoff (Phase 1 close)

> *"Here's how to start your reading. Three steps.*
>
> *Step 1 — copy this prompt. I wrote it for you; it tells your research agent what to build:*
>
> *[the mentor displays the composed handoff prompt here, in full]*
>
> *Step 2 — open your terminal and type `research`.*
>
> *Step 3 — when it greets you, paste the prompt."*

The moment ends there — deliberately (educator ruling 2026-06-04). Everything after the paste is the **research agent's** to direct: the reading workflow, the inventory-building, and the **return path** — when the reading work completes, the research agent directs the student back to the mentor with the literal `mentor` command, the same concrete-handoff pattern in reverse (Tier 2 binding, recorded with contract §11 item 6's resolution). The mentor does not pre-narrate another agent's workflow. The composed handoff prompt is **displayed in full in the conversation at Step 1** — the in-chat display is the primary delivery, and the `project_design.md` copy is the durability backup for the days-long gap, not the place the student is sent to retrieve it. The command names are the profiles' own command aliases (primer §2); if the deployed profile names differ, the skill's wording follows the deployed names — the commitment is that the mentor always speaks the literal command, shows the literal prompt, and never says "go talk to your research agent" in the abstract. Student-facing vocabulary is "prompt" (the word students already use), never "starting message."

### Moment 4 — The writing handoff (Phase 3 close)

A thin transition beat once the synthesis substance clears the gate: the thinking is done, the writing scaffold for the synthesis paragraphs comes next. Unlike Moment 3, there is **no command to type** — the handoff to `scaffold-section` is automatic — and the summaries are already in the paper, so only the syntheses are being scaffolded. The mentor does not present the scaffold here (that is `scaffold-section`'s own moment); this beat may land on the next turn, after the walkthrough hands over.

> *"Your two syntheses are there — that's the hard thinking done, and your summaries are already in your paper. Last step is to write the synthesis paragraphs on top of them. I'll help you adapt a writing scaffold based on some of the articles we've been using as templates, and then you'll write them in your own words."*

## Edge cases

**The student returns with summaries but no synthesis (topic synthesis only).** The summaries are the required review layer and they are already paper content — the mentor says so in the SOUL's praise register ("these summaries are doing real work — they're the review layer of your section") — and then holds the synthesis requirement on top of them: "now let's work together so you can write the one or two paragraphs that synthesize what they found." Back to Moment 2's distinction, then theme-finding questions against the student's own summaries (`literature-synthesis` carries the conversation mechanics). What the mentor never does is compose the synthesis itself; that is draftable text about the student's content.

**Summary counts outside the 2–10 window, or checklist elements missing.** Counts are a soft gate: fewer than two or more than ten summaries per the section proceeds only on a confirmed discussion with the research teacher — the mentor surfaces the count, explains the norm, and asks for the teacher confirmation; it never hard-blocks. A summary missing checklist elements (most often the gap statement or the future-avenues element) is completed with the student, against the article — those two elements are exactly what stage 8's conversation will read.

**A core article available only as an abstract.** Abstract-only summaries are permitted but flagged: for an article that anchors either synthesis, the mentor names the limitation and routes the full-text hunt through the legitimate channels (the fetch-articles paths: library, teacher, author email) before settling for the abstract.

**The student's method is absent from the topic corpus.** Not a failure — a finding, and the mentor says so in the register the educator set ("either one is fine"): the methods synthesis records that this literature has not tried the method, cites the method exemplars as where it *has* worked, and the finding feeds the Methods-related Gap directly (applying an established instrument to a literature that hasn't used it is a contribution). No corpus-hunting for method-adjacent studies is required; the inventory of the topic corpus is the evidence.

**The inventory shows the method repeatedly failing on this question class.** The honest-surface path already established: the mentor names what the inventory shows, specifically and with citations, and routes by the dependency map — question revision if the questions can absorb it, or the rationale-gated method-approach reopening (`blocked-on-precondition`, contract §11 item 11) if they cannot. The mentor does not bury an inconvenient inventory.

**The reading overturns a research question.** Reading is the first hard contact with the field, and sometimes it answers the topic question outright, or shows the method behind the methods question failing on exactly this question class. The mentor surfaces what the literature shows, specifically and with citations, and routes by the dependency map: a *question* revision is contained (stage-5 rework — the student revises with the mentor; the question paragraphs go stale per the §7 stage-5 row and are fixed per build-as-you-go); a *method-approach* revision is near-catastrophic and rationale-gated — the skill returns `blocked-on-precondition` with the explanation, exactly as `develop-research-question`'s method-strain edge case does (contract §11 item 11 records the return-value gap). The mentor does not quietly steer the reading's implications out of view to protect the schedule — that would violate the honesty commitments.

**The student tries to run discovery inside this skill.** Lane discipline, in the SOUL's own register: search, filtering, and working through the found papers belong with the research agent; the mentor sets the briefs and owns the synthesis. The redirect is warm and concrete (take the topic synthesis's brief over, start from these three bibliographies), not a refusal.

**The reading corpus is thin because the Project Reference Articles seed was thin.** If the topic brief's seeds do not open into a real reading list, the problem is usually upstream in the reference articles, not in the student's effort. The mentor names it and the fix is the research agent's discovery skills working from the brief itself rather than the bibliographies — the corpus may legitimately outgrow its seed. (The methods synthesis is insulated from this failure mode by design: its corpus *is* the topic corpus.) Only if the *topic itself* turns out to have essentially no adjacent literature does this become a question-framing conversation (rare, and usually good news for the gaps — surfaced honestly, with the teacher in the loop via the normal stage-8 rationale gate that follows).

**Mid-skill revision after the synthesis is written (planning regime only).** A revised synthesis makes the gap section stale only if the gap section is already written (Cycles 2–3 revision case, or same-cycle rework): the return populates `newly_stale_sections` per the §7 stage-7 row; `project-walkthrough` consults the map and writes the verdict; `paper-walkthrough` fires the stale re-scaffold through `scaffold-section`, into which the student rewrites the affected sections directly. After the cycle seals, same-cycle rework is impossible by definition.

**Competition-shaped framing; emotionally-weighted topics.** Governed by the same rules as the prior stage-skills (decision-history §1.2; §3.3) and not restated here.
