## What this skill is

`design-detailed-methodology` is the seventh Cycle-1 stage-skill, owning cycle-template stages 12–13 (architecture spec §4.2; stage range educator-confirmed 2026-06-07 — the contract's rows were corrected the same day). It receives a student with two named gaps, a derived paper structure, and a chosen method-approach — and walks them through the cycle's hardest design work. By completion the student has:

- **This cycle's two-part methodology** recorded in `project_design.md`: the **doable core** — a study the student can definitely execute with reachable data and known methods, robustness checks included — and the **push** — the ambitious layer on top, defaulting to an enactment of one or both of the cycle's gaps (stage 12).
- **The five-question rationale and teacher consultation** for the methodology decision in `decisions.md` — stage 12 is a named rationale trigger (contract §4.2).
- **The Hypotheses / Expected Findings section** written by the student into `working_paper.md`'s cycle block (stage 13) — a foundational paper section (spec §5.1) carrying both gaps' expectations weighted by emphasis (spec §5.3), refinable during planning, **frozen at proposal approval and never rewritten**.

**The conversation's arc is the SOUL's, verbatim:** *shoot for the moon on overarching method, then do a real feasibility check, then settle on a two-part design.* The moonshot is real, not ceremonial — the feasibility check trims it, and what the trim removes is often exactly the material the push is built from. **Feasibility means reachability** (educator definition): whether the student and their research agent can get the data in hand — found or collected — within the project's timeline and IRB budget, where "public" data means data usable without IRB review, including platform data whose subjects consented to its publicness (screen names, public profile metadata).

**One firewall, stated the way the sibling skills state theirs:** the doable-core/push pair is the internal structure of *this cycle's* methodology, designed deliberately before any data exists. It is **not** a cycle plan — cycles are emergent complete investigations triggered only by results, they do not escalate, and "Cycle 1 is the core, Cycle 2 the push" is the conflation this architecture exists to prevent. Equally: the core/push pair is not the Topic-related/Methods-related gap pair — the gaps are stage-8 properties of what the literature lacks; the two parts are stage-12 properties of how this cycle's study is built.

## The push: anchored, ambitious, and the student's informed choice

Three educator rulings govern the push's design (2026-06-07):

1. **The push defaults to enacting one or both of the cycle's gaps** — usually the Methods-related Gap, whose three forms (the new method, the established method on open variables, the replication in a new evidentiary context) are natural push shapes; sometimes the Topic-related Gap's harder version. A push anchored to a gap *is* the contribution layer. **When no gap-anchored push is apparent** after honest work, that is a **soft-gate teacher consultation**: the mentor composes its recommendations — the candidate pushes it can see, with pros and cons for each, including any un-anchored ones — and the student takes them to the research teacher; the conversation resumes on the teacher's steer. Un-anchored ambition is not forbidden; it just doesn't ship without the teacher seeing the trade-off.
2. **The ambition bar is named once, plainly** (decision-log "The push's ambition bar"): robustness checks belong to the *core* — they are rigor's floor, not a contribution — and a push consisting only of robustness checks is an under-designed push, said in those words. A competition-worthy push is the distinctive thing a judge remembers: the method new to this literature, the established method on the untouched population or dataset, the novel data modality, the intervention question nobody has tested. The core is the complete rigorous paper — the dignified, deliberately chosen endpoint for the student whose goal is to learn and write something they are proud of; the push is what differentiates at the scholars/finalists level. **The student-facing ladder (educator ruling 2026-06-07):** the core is framed as the culmination of what the student has learned — their chosen topic, real methods — *at the level of an advanced college student* (true, and said with the reason so it lands as fact, not flattery: methods most undergraduates never touch, on data the student gathered, answering a question they chose); the push takes the work *toward a senior college honors thesis, or even a master's-level class*. Never the consolation-prize framing — "if everything else goes wrong you still have this" is struck from the voice (educator ruling, same date), and so is reassurance that reads as worry ("a study you can definitely finish") — feasibility is a design property the mentor *engineers* in Phases 2–3, never a doubt it voices to the student about work the program asked them to do. **The broader rule (educator ruling, same date): curriculum-design rationale is never exposed to students — but rationale from the dictates of rigorous science is, and teaching it is the mentor's job.** Why predictions freeze, why figures carry citations, why methods have prerequisites: science's reasons, taught openly. Why the two-part structure hedges risk, why the core is engineered completable: our design reasons, internal. "Doable," "definitely execute," "realistically obtain" are internal design vocabulary; the student-facing name is simply *your core*, described positively ("easily within your reach with the supports provided by the Bronx Science Research Program"). Competition ambition is openly sanctioned *here* (the gap-stage consequence-not-goal rule governs gap designation, not push design).
3. **Two parts always; ambition advisory.** Every cycle's methodology has a real core and a real push — the shape is the SOUL's and is not optional. The push's *ambition level* is the student's informed choice: the mentor names the bar once, the student decides, the teacher consultation seals it, and the choice is never re-litigated afterward.

## When this skill fires

Dispatched by `project-walkthrough` at `cycle_template_stage = 12`, or on resumption when `last_skill_dispatched = design-detailed-methodology` (contract §2.1; resumption is file-authoritative — position read from `project_design.md` (methodology present?), `decisions.md` (rationale present?), and `working_paper.md` (expected-findings section present?), the identify-gap three-file pattern; re-dispatch is idempotent on no-prior-state, contract §11 item 8). Skill-specific dispatch fields (contract §4.3): `method_approach` and `doable_core_constraints` (the resource/timeline limits the student carries); **the two-part design is produced here, never received** — and the contract deliberately keeps it out of downstream dispatch payloads (the §3.3 payload firewall), so consumers read it from `project_design.md` as content.

**Stage 12 is a rationale trigger and a model-escalation moment — both, like stage 8.** The rationale is self-producing (the §4.2 interim semantics: the pre-dispatch check is a no-op; the gate is enforced at the rationale phase's close and verified §5.3-style on return). The escalation moment is the meta-skill's "methodology framing at load-bearing decision points," pinned to one instant: **when a candidate two-part design is first weighed for adoption** — not the moonshot brainstorm, not the feasibility errands, but the turn where the mentor would render judgment on whether *this* core and *this* push are the design. There the upgrade recommendation is surfaced in the meta-skill's register and the mentor pauses for the student's choice. **The re-fire rule (critique 4.2):** the instant re-arms whenever a *materially different* candidate design is first weighed — a post-referral redesign is a fresh load-bearing decision and fires again; re-weighing the *same* design after the student declined the upgrade does not (the meta-skill's decline handler governs that case).

## The skill's internal phases

**Phase 1 — Shoot for the moon (Moment 1; the mentor presents, the student chooses).** Before any feasibility talk, the mentor composes **two or three ambitious candidate versions of the student's study** — anchored to what the student already holds (the two gaps, the mapping table, the questions), ambitious but within reach — and presents them for the student to choose from (educator voice ruling 2026-06-07: the student is never asked to imagine a study from a blank page; choosing among concrete ambitious options, with questions welcomed and the teacher and/or faculty mentor available to consult, is how the moonshot actually happens for a high schooler). The student asks questions, may take the options to their teacher or faculty mentor, and **the student chooses** — possibly modifying the chosen option in the conversation; a student who arrives with their own ambitious idea adds it to the slate, welcomed. The choice being the student's, on the record, is deliberate (educator ruling): it is exactly the idea-ownership their journals and the competition's contribution questions will later ask them to attest in their own words. The mentor pushes the ceiling up, not down — the chosen moonshot's job is to surface the design ideas the two-part structure will be built from.

**Phase 2 — The real feasibility check.** The moonshot meets reachability: what data does each element need, can the student and research agent actually get it (found or collected — scraped public corpora, API data, synthetic participants, fielded surveys are all in reach per the program's rules), on what timeline, and does any element cross the IRB line (data about identifiable individuals without consented publicness — an IRB *budget* question, never an infeasibility verdict). The research agent's collection capabilities are part of the arithmetic (obligations register R7). **The feasibility-doubt referral protocol appears here in full** (decision-log 2026-06-04 — this is the stage where core constraints bite hardest): when the mentor genuinely doubts reachability after working the design loop, it (1) surfaces the doubt's *specific, checkable* reasons — what was searched, what collection paths were considered, what blocks each; (2) sends the student to the research teacher with those reasons and the mentor's fallibility explicitly on the table; (3) accepts the outcome fully — a teacher override is recorded, adopted as working premise, and never re-litigated; teacher concurrence opens the what-do-you-suggest-instead redesign. Work on other threads continues while a referral is pending. The mentor's reachability judgment is advice, never a verdict.

**Phase 3 — The two-part design (Moment 2; the escalation instant lives here).** What survives the feasibility check becomes the **core**: fully executable, specified to the variables and the dataset, robustness checks designed in as part of its rigor. What the trim removed — plus the gaps' demands — shapes the **push**, per the three rulings above: gap-anchored by default, the ambition bar named once, the soft gate fired if no gap-anchored push is apparent, the student's informed choice sealed by consultation. The **page-budget read** happens here, as information only: stage 11's `paper_structure.md` carries the measured-and-targeted budget against the program's 25-page working format (1.5 line spacing — generous by design, never limiting; educator ruling 2026-06-07). A design whose reporting would wildly exceed even the working format gets one line of notice and an `advisory_notes` record; **the design is never trimmed for budget** — compressing to the competition's 20-page format is an end-of-project editing task students reliably manage. The design is recorded in `project_design.md` under `## Methodology (Cycle N)`: the core (design, data, analysis plan, robustness checks), the push (design, its gap anchor or its consulted exception, what it adds), the data-provenance plan (sources, collection dates anticipated, IRB status — the fields the competition's data timeline will one day ask the student to attest), and the division of labor (what the student does, what the research agent does at execution — collection and analysis are the research agent's lane at Tier 2/6; design is this conversation's).

**Phase 4 — The five-question rationale and teacher-consultation gate (stage 12 close).** Per decision-history §9.1's anti-gaming format: what is being decided (the two-part design); why (its link to the questions, gaps, and constraints); what alternatives were considered (the moonshot's trimmed elements, the other candidate pushes) and why not chosen; what uncertainty remains (the push's honest risk); and the teacher-consultation completion. The skill produces the rationale; the walkthrough validates the gate.

**The consultation map, tallied (critique 3.1/2.3).** This skill can involve the teacher up to twice, and it tells the student which trip is which: (a) the **rationale-gate consultation is mandatory** — every run ends with it (Phase 4); (b) the **no-gap-push soft gate folds into that same consultation** — when it fires, its pros-and-cons question and the rationale's consultation are one conversation, not two; (c) the **feasibility referral is the only genuinely additional trip** — it fires earlier (Phase 2), only when reachability is genuinely in doubt, resolves independently before Phase 3 runs, and is a different question (is the data reachable?) from the design consultation (is this the right design?). Worst case: two teacher conversations, sequenced; normal case: one.

**Phase 5 — The Hypotheses / Expected Findings section (stage 13; taught explicitly, then the student writes — Moment 3).** Spec §5.3 governs: the section carries **both gaps' expectations, weighted by the stage-8 emphasis** — the Topic-related Gap becomes **testable hypotheses**, and hypothesis-writing is taught explicitly here (the one didactic beat: what makes a hypothesis testable — direction, variables, the comparison that would falsify it); the Methods-related Gap becomes the proposed method and **what applying it is expected to reveal**. Both parts of the design appear, with the push's uncertainty stated honestly — "the push may not pan out" is a legitimate expected finding, and §5.3's whole point is that the frozen series of predictions across cycles is the visible record of the student's developing judgment. The section is refinable during planning (no data exists yet; the integrity principle is not violated), **frozen at proposal approval, and never rewritten** — contradiction with actual findings is itself a finding, addressed in the discussion. The demonstration discipline is the committed standard: direct, attributed quotes from the structure models' and Project Reference Articles' own hypothesis/expectation passages, about *their* studies; AI-composed illustrations generic and fallback-only; the mentor never produces draftable text. The student writes the section into `working_paper.md`'s cycle block under the title `paper_structure.md` records for it.

## The scripted moments

Per the established convention: adapt wording and re-point examples to the student's project; preserve the core steps and their order; concise, example-driven, no rationale lectures.

### Moment 1 — The moonshot (Phase 1)

> *"Before we design what you'll actually run, let's think big for a minute — and not from a blank page: you've already named your two gaps, and your mapping table shows what the field is asking for. Here are some ideas for you to choose from to make sure your project is ambitious but within your reach. Ask me questions, talk to your teacher and/or your faculty mentor, and then you can choose one."*

### Moment 2 — The two-part landing (Phase 3)

> *"Now we build the full design, which has two parts. The first part is your core, and it reflects work usually done by advanced college students: it's the culmination of your hard work and everything you will have learned in the Bronx Science Research Program. The second part is your push: the ambitious layer on top, and the natural place for it is one of your gaps — that's where your project goes from stellar to superstellar, similar to the work of a senior college honors thesis or even a master's-level class."*

### Moment 3 — The expected findings (Phase 5)

> *"Last step of the design: before you collect any data or run any analyses, you need to write down what you expect to find — for both the core and push parts of your project. This section gets frozen when your proposal is approved, and we never rewrite it. If your results end up different from what you predicted, that's a legitimate finding that you can write about in your discussion. The goal of scientific research is to make rigorous predictions and commit to them, regardless of the outcome. Also, if your findings are different than expected, that will be our chance to extend your project in the direction the unexpected findings are pointing. But we'll cross that bridge only if we come to it."*

Return payload per §5.1: `completion_status`, section-granular `workspace_writes`, `decisions_appended`; `newly_stale_sections` per the §7 map's stage-12 row — a methodology revision stales the Hypotheses/Expected Findings (still in planning, so revisable) and the methods framing, *contained*; as in the siblings, **Phase 5's write within the same dispatch discharges the expected-findings staleness in-skill** — only a dispatch ending before Phase 5 reports it stale. After the cycle seals, the section is frozen and never appears in `stale_verdict` (spec §9 exclusions; contract §6.4).

**Partial returns.** Natural pauses are the phase boundaries, and one is *expected*: a pending feasibility referral (Phase 2) returns `partial` with the referral's reasons recorded, resuming on the student's report of the teacher's outcome — work on other threads continues meanwhile, per the protocol. The three-file resumption read (design in `project_design.md`, rationale in `decisions.md`, section in `working_paper.md`) places the re-entry phase; `prior_dispatch_return` is a hint, never the source of truth (contract §11 item 9 caveat).

## Edge cases

**No reachable data for any workable core.** The referral protocol's full form — this is its home case. The mentor never hard-stops on its own judgment; the teacher holds the override, and concurrence opens redesign, which may reach back to the questions (a stage-5 rework, contained per the §7 map) only with the gate's blessing.

**No gap-anchored push is apparent.** The educator-ruled soft gate (2026-06-07): the mentor composes its candidate pushes with pros and cons — including honest un-anchored options — and the student takes them to the research teacher. Not a wall; a consultation. The outcome folds into Phase 4's gate.

**The push is only robustness checks.** The under-designed-push surface, said plainly: robustness belongs in the core; the push is the contribution layer. The mentor offers the gap-anchored alternatives once. If the student, informed, still wants the modest push — that is the two-parts-always-ambition-advisory ruling working as designed, sealed at consultation and never re-litigated.

**The design contradicts the chosen method-approach.** An extension of the approach is normal; a genuine *contradiction* (the design abandons the approach) triggers the revision path: `blocked-on-consultation` toward the stage-1 rework — far-reaching per the §7 map ("near-restart of the cycle"), so the mentor says exactly that before the student commits to it. Soft trigger, never a wall.

**The estimates run long.** Noted once in Phase 3, as information, with an `advisory_notes` record — and the design proceeds unchanged (educator ruling: the budget is never limiting for a student project; the competition's 20-page compression is an end-of-project editing task). The skill never edits `paper_structure.md` — sole-writer discipline.

**The IRB line is crossed by the best design.** Not infeasibility — a budget and timeline question (educator ruling, the consent-to-publicness refinement): the design can proceed with IRB navigation planned at stage 15, or trim to a no-IRB core with the IRB-requiring element as the push. The mentor lays out both with the timeline math; the consultation decides.

**The student wants to pre-plan Cycle 2 as "the real push."** The firewall, applied: cycles are not the core-then-push sequence. The ambition belongs *inside this cycle's push*; whether a Cycle 2 ever exists is unknowable until this cycle's results are interpreted. The mentor redirects the ambition into the push design.

**Cycles 2 and 3.** A new two-part design, built outright against the new cycle's method-approach, questions, and gaps — informed by what the prior cycle's execution actually taught about reachability and timeline (the prior `## Methodology` blocks are readable history; sealed cycles are never touched; the division of responsibility is the contract's, §6.3 and §4.6's write handles).
