## What this skill is

`assemble-proposal` is the eighth Cycle-1 stage-skill, owning cycle-template stage 14 (architecture spec §4.2, §8). It receives a student whose planning is complete — methodology designed (stage 12), Hypotheses and/or Expected Findings written (stage 13) — and carries them through the cycle's last planning act: the proposal and the teacher sign-off that seals the cycle. By completion the student has:

- **The compiled proposal** in `proposals/cycle-N-proposal.md`: the cycle's already-written sections assembled into one short document — the research problem paragraph (Cycle 1 only), the research question paragraphs, the Literature Review and Synthesis section, the gap section, the Hypotheses and/or Expected Findings, and the detailed methodology — plus the student's **forward-looking paragraphs** anticipating IRB, data collection, and analysis.
- **The five-question rationale and teacher sign-off** in `decisions.md` — stage 14 is a named rationale trigger (contract §4.2), and **this is the single most important teacher checkpoint in each cycle** (spec §8): the last cheap moment to catch a flawed design before the expensive, hard-to-reverse work of execution.
- **The sealed cycle.** Proposal approval is the cycle seal (spec §6): the **predictions and the planning framing** freeze (the Hypotheses and/or Expected Findings and the question/gap/lit-review framing — so the before/after honesty is on the record), `current_regime` becomes execution, and the project moves to IRB and data collection. The seal freezes *what was predicted and how the study was framed* — it does **not** freeze the conduct of the study: the methods, results, and discussion are written during execution as new content, and legitimate execution-time adaptation (operationalization refined against the data, a collection contingency, an analysis choice the data forces) is normal and is reported honestly in the methods 'as executed' (spec §6, stage 16). What the seal prevents is the dishonest act — rewriting predictions to match results — not the honest one of adapting the protocol and reporting the change.

**This is a compile-plus-extrapolate step, not a fresh Socratic skill** (spec §8). Almost all the proposal's content already exists — the skill *assembles* it; it does not regenerate it, and it never edits the source sections (it compiles copies). The only new writing the student does is the forward-looking paragraphs, and even those are short. The skill is architecturally closer to `compile-working-paper` than to the design conversations upstream.

**The forward-looking paragraphs are reasoned expectations to test, not hypotheses to defend** (spec §8) — and they are **proposal-only**: they anticipate how IRB will go, how collection will run, what analysis will involve, in the light available before execution. At execution (stages 15–17) the student writes the *real* methods, results, and discussion "as executed," informed by these paragraphs but never bound to them; the paragraphs are never frozen as paper sections (that role belongs to the Hypotheses and/or Expected Findings section, stage 13). They feed the IRB submission where one is needed — writing the proposal *is* starting the IRB application (spec §8, §9.4).

## When this skill fires

Dispatched by `project-walkthrough` at `cycle_template_stage = 14`, or on resumption when `last_skill_dispatched = assemble-proposal` (contract §2.1; resumption is file-authoritative — position read from `proposals/cycle-N-proposal.md` (compiled? forward paragraphs present?) and `decisions.md` (consultation chain complete?); re-dispatch is idempotent on no-prior-state, contract §11 item 8). Skill-specific dispatch fields (contract §4.3): `compiled_section_handles` (the already-written sections to compile) and `consultation_chain_state` (where the student is in the five-question rationale and teacher-consultation sequence).

**Stage 14 is a rationale trigger** (contract §4.2) — the heaviest one. Like the other self-producing triggers, the §4.2 interim semantics hold: the pre-dispatch check is a no-op, the gate is enforced at the consultation chain's close, and verified §5.3-style on return (a proposal claiming sign-off with no matching `decisions.md` consultation record is treated as `partial`, never sealed). **Stage 14 is also a model-escalation moment** — and the meta-skill's *clearest* one: **proposal review** is the first item on the meta-skill's closed list ("the highest-stakes mentor act and the clearest case for the upgrade"), and `assemble-proposal` is the first-named skill on its integration roster. The instant is pinned, the way the siblings pin theirs: **the mentor's evaluative review of the assembled proposal against the Regenerrific standard** — Phase 3, before the rationale and sign-off, when the mentor reads the whole proposal as a judge would and renders its assessment. The *compilation* (Phase 1) and the forward-paragraph teaching beat (Phase 2) are explicitly **off-moment** — mechanical assembly and a single didactic beat, not evaluative judgment, the way design-detailed-methodology fences its moonshot and feasibility errands off its escalation instant. At the pinned instant the upgrade recommendation is surfaced in the meta-skill's register and the mentor pauses for the student's choice before the proposal goes to sign-off; the skill references `model-escalation` rather than re-implementing the recommendation (the meta-skill's single-source rule).

## The skill's internal phases

**Phase 1 — Compile (mechanical; Moment 1 opens it).** The skill assembles the cycle's already-written sections into `proposals/cycle-N-proposal.md` in proposal order: research problem (Cycle 1), research questions, Literature Review and Synthesis, gap, Hypotheses and/or Expected Findings, detailed methodology. These are **copies** — the compile never edits the sources in `working_paper.md` and `project_design.md` (contract §4.6; the proposal file is the only thing this skill writes besides the `decisions.md` rationale). A precondition check first: every section the proposal needs must exist and be current; a missing or stale upstream section is a contract bug reaching this skill (the walkthrough's §4.4-style precondition check should have caught it), surfaced as `blocked-on-precondition` rather than compiled around.

**Phase 2 — The forward-looking paragraphs (the student writes; one teaching beat).** The student writes short paragraphs anticipating the three execution stages: how IRB review is expected to go (if applicable — IRB is needed only if the project collects data directly from human subjects, per the methodology's IRB-status field); how data collection is expected to run (sources, timeline, the research agent's role); what the analysis is expected to involve. The register is taught once: these are **reasoned expectations to test, not promises** — written in the same honest-prediction spirit as the expected findings, and just as safe to be wrong about, but a *different document* that is never frozen (the frozen paper section is the stage-13 Hypotheses and/or Expected Findings; these forward paragraphs live only in the proposal). The demonstration discipline is the committed standard (decision-history §8.1): the mentor may show how published proposals or methods sections frame anticipated work, via attributed quotes from the Project Reference Articles; the student writes their own sentences; the mentor never drafts proposal prose.

**Phase 3 — The mentor's proposal review (the escalation instant), then the five-question rationale and full consultation chain (the hard gate; stage 14 close).** This phase opens with the **escalation instant**: before the rationale, the mentor reads the assembled proposal *as a judge would* — engagement, methodological sophistication, the advanced-college-level bar, all at once — and renders its assessment. This is proposal review, the meta-skill's highest-stakes case (see "When this skill fires"); the mentor references `model-escalation` here and pauses for the student's upgrade choice before the evaluative read, because this is exactly the multi-criteria judgment the upgrade exists for. Then the five-question rationale, per decision-history §9.1: what is being decided (proceeding to execution on this design); why (the design is complete and sound); what alternatives were considered (the design's own trimmed options, already recorded — the proposal rationale references rather than relitigates them); what uncertainty remains (the honest risks the execution will test); and the teacher-consultation completion. **This is the cycle's most important checkpoint** — the teacher sign-off here is the last cheap review before execution, and the five-question gate's anti-gaming form (talk-or-email, one sentence on what the teacher said, per §9.1) applies in full. The mentor's review and the teacher's sign-off are distinct: the mentor's evaluative read is advice that strengthens the proposal *before* it reaches the teacher; the teacher's sign-off is the hard gate. The skill produces the rationale and the proposal; the walkthrough validates the chain and performs the seal.

**Phase 4 — The seal (Moment 2; the milestone named honestly).** Before the sign-off lands, the mentor names the seal plainly (Moment 2): approval freezes the **predictions and the framing** and moves the project into execution — the last point to change the *framing* before data collection, while the *conduct* of the study stays free to meet reality (see the legitimate-execution-change edge case). This is a **milestone, not a trap**: the freeze is what makes the before/after structure of real research possible (spec §6), and the student crosses it having done the work, not under warning. On the teacher's approval, the skill returns `seal_state_change` set (contract §5.1, §7) — the only skill that ever sets it — and the walkthrough performs the irreversible seal transition (§7's load-bearing write order). The mentor is honest that the seal is irreversible within the system (only an educator intervention could unseal), which is exactly why the checkpoint matters.

## The scripted moments

Per the established convention: adapt wording and re-point examples to the student's project; preserve the core steps and their order; concise, example-driven, no rationale lectures.

### Moment 1 — Opening the proposal (Phase 1)

> *"You've designed the whole study — now we put it together into one proposal: your question, your literature review, your gap, what you expect to find, and your methods, all in one place. Most of it you've already written; I'll assemble it. The new part is short — a few paragraphs on what you expect from the practical steps ahead: getting any approvals you need, collecting your data, running your analysis."*

### Moment 2 — The seal (Phase 4)

> *"This is the checkpoint. When your teacher approves this proposal, your predictions and your framing lock in — that's what keeps your science honest, because later you can't pretend you predicted whatever you happen to find. From here you move from planning to running your study. Your plan can still meet reality: if you have to adjust how you collect or measure something once you're in the data, that's normal, and you'll simply report what you actually did. And if your data show you something surprising you never predicted, that's a real finding too — you report it openly as something you discovered, not as something you'd planned all along. What's fixed is what you predicted; what's always open is what you're allowed to discover."*

## What this skill writes to the workspace

`proposals/cycle-N-proposal.md`: the compiled proposal + the forward-looking paragraphs (Phases 1–2) — the skill is a sanctioned writer of `proposals/` per contract §4.6 (added 2026-06-07). `decisions.md`: the stage-14 five-question rationale and the consultation-chain record (Phase 3), named in the return payload's `decisions_appended`. **Nothing to `working_paper.md`** — the proposal compiles copies, never edits the paper's sections (the forward-looking paragraphs are proposal-only, not paper content). Return payload per contract §5.1: `completion_status`, `workspace_writes` (the proposal file, the decisions append), `decisions_appended`, and — uniquely — **`seal_state_change`** set to (current cycle, sealed) on approval; `null` until then. `newly_stale_sections` is empty: stage 14 writes no paper section and the §7 map has no stage-14 row.

**Partial returns.** The natural pauses are the phase boundaries, and one is *expected and lengthy*: the consultation chain (Phase 3) is a teacher checkpoint that genuinely pauses the dispatch — a return with the proposal compiled and the forward paragraphs written but the teacher not yet consulted is `partial`, resuming on the student's report of the sign-off. Resumption is file-authoritative (proposal file present? forward paragraphs in it? consultation record in `decisions.md`?); `prior_dispatch_return` is a hint, never the source of truth (contract §11 item 8). **The seal is never partial in a way that loses safety**: per §7's write order, `seal_status` is written last, so any failure before it leaves the cycle still in planning, the proposal-return integration simply re-running — the validator catches and recovers any half-written transition.

## Edge cases

**An upstream section is missing or stale at compile time.** A contract bug, not a runtime case to design around (the walkthrough's precondition check owns it) — surfaced as `blocked-on-precondition` with the specific section named, never compiled around with a placeholder. The proposal is only as sound as the sections it assembles, so it refuses to assemble an incomplete cycle.

**The teacher does not approve — the proposal comes back for revision.** The most important thing this skill protects, working as designed: this is the cheap catch the whole checkpoint exists for. The teacher's concern routes to whichever stage owns the flagged element — a methodology problem reopens stage 12, a gap problem stage 8, a question problem stage 5 — via the dependency map and the `blocked-on-precondition`/upstream-reopening path (contract §11 item 11). The cycle does **not** seal; `seal_state_change` stays `null`; the student returns to the named stage, fixes it, and comes back. No work is lost; the proposal recompiles from the revised sections. The mentor frames a revision request as the checkpoint succeeding, not the student failing.

**The student wants to skip ahead to data collection before sign-off.** The hard gate, stated plainly: the proposal approval *is* the permission to collect data (spec §8), and there is no path to execution around it. Not a soft nudge — the architecture does not advance `current_regime` to execution without the seal. The mentor explains *why* in science's terms (the proposal is the pre-registration-like commitment that makes the findings credible — a science rationale, student-facing per the voice rule), never as a bureaucratic block.

**IRB is required.** Not a complication — the proposal is *designed* to feed the IRB submission (spec §8; decision-history §9.4 frames the IRB approach): the forward-looking IRB paragraph and the methodology's human-subjects details are the start of the IRB application. The mentor frames the overlap as work that does double duty, and the timeline math (IRB review takes weeks) is surfaced here so the student plans for it — the IRB-status field set at stage 12 already records whether this path applies.

**The student asks, during or after the seal, whether they can change something.** The first-class distinction this skill teaches, and the answer is almost always yes: a change to *how the study is run* — operationalization refined when the data arrives, a measure adjusted, a collection path that has to shift, an analysis decision the data forces — is legitimate, expected, and honest science, written into the methods 'as executed' with the deviation from plan reported (and the discussion noting why). What the seal forbids is the *one dishonest act*: reaching back to rewrite the frozen predictions or the framing so they match what was found (HARKing / p-hacking). The test the mentor gives the student, in science's own terms (a student-facing rationale per the voice rule): "are you changing what you *did*, or changing what you *said you'd predict*? The first is research meeting reality — report it. The second is the thing the freeze exists to prevent." A student scared into a rigid protocol by the word "freeze" is a failure mode the mentor actively heads off — the seal protects honesty, it does not forbid adaptation.

**The data reveal something the student never predicted (an unexpected result).** Distinct from the protocol-change case above: here the *findings*, not the conduct, are the surprise. The honest choice is the **confirmatory/exploratory distinction** — the frozen predictions were the confirmatory test and stand as written; an unforeseen pattern is pursued and reported as a **clearly labeled exploratory finding** (discovered, not predicted), never folded back into the frozen section as though it had been anticipated. The mentor frames this as a strength, not a problem: open science values an honestly labeled exploratory result and is unimpressed by a prediction quietly rewritten to fit. If the unexpected result opens a *genuinely new question* worth its own investigation, that is the `assess-new-cycle` trigger (report the current cycle first, then decide) — not a reason to edit the sealed cycle.

**Cycles 2 and 3.** A new proposal for the new cycle, compiling that cycle's own sections (no research problem paragraph — it is once-only, Cycle 1's), with its own forward-looking paragraphs and its own seal. Prior cycles' proposals stand untouched in `proposals/`; sealed cycles are never reopened — the division of responsibility is the contract's (§6.3, §7's irreversibility), and this skill seals only the current cycle.

## Where this skill lives in the architecture

A **project-track stage-skill** dispatched by `project-walkthrough`, bundled in the project-mentor distribution (curator-protected, update-refreshed per platform primer §7). It is the **only skill that sets `seal_state_change`** (contract §5.1, §7) — the architectural pivot between planning and execution regimes. It writes `proposals/cycle-N-proposal.md` and `decisions.md` only (contract §4.6); it is both a rationale trigger (§4.2) and a `model-escalation` moment (proposal review — the meta-skill's clearest, highest-stakes case; the pinned instant is the mentor's evaluative review of the assembled proposal, Phase 3). Upstream it consumes every planning-regime output (the foundational sections, the methodology, the expected findings); downstream, the seal it triggers opens the execution regime — IRB (stage 15), data collection (stage 16), analysis (stage 17), all in the research agent's lane (spec §12), running the design the proposal froze. It is the last planning skill; `assess-new-cycle` (the between-cycles transition) is the only project-track skill after the execution stages.
